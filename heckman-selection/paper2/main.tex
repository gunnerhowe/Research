\begin{abstract}
Learning-curve surrogates for hyperparameter optimization --- freeze-thaw
GPs, power-law extrapolation, in-context learning-curve networks --- are
fit and deployed on curves that \emph{survived} earlier rungs of
successive halving (SH), Hyperband, or ASHA, yet no method in this line
models the censoring mechanism. We state the honest crux up front:
selection acts on \emph{observed} prefixes, so if prefixes were noiseless
the censoring would be ignorable (missing at random). The bias enters
because prefixes are noisy observations of latent curve parameters: rung
survival selects on noise, so survivors' continuations systematically
disappoint a naive per-curve extrapolation --- regression-to-the-mean
survivor bias with a Tobit/Heckman structure on the latent parameters. In
a controlled power-law family the effect is precisely dose-responsive:
survivor selection bias at the first rung grows from \GateBiasSigLo{}
(accuracy units) at noise $\sigma = 0.005$ to \GateBiasSigXhi{} at
$\sigma = 0.04$ and grows with selection pressure $\eta$; at the
\emph{median} noise we calibrate from real LCBench curves
($\sigma \approx \CalibSigDiff$) it is \emph{indistinguishable from zero}
--- an informative null about SH robustness that we report rather than
bury, with the caveat that noisier regimes (small validation sets;
PD1's ImageNet task at $\sigma \approx 0.02$) sit well inside the
biased zone. A Heckman-type joint selection+outcome model --- a
hierarchical prior over latent curve parameters with rung-survival probit
likelihoods --- attacks the survivor bias but exposes the same
identification fragility econometrics warns of: with a single SH bracket
it is weakly identified (it prefers the biased solution at realistic
survivor counts), and pooling three Hyperband-style brackets ---
rung-assignment randomness as an \emph{exclusion restriction} --- cuts the
survivor-only asymptote bias from $+0.084$ to $-0.036$ accuracy units
(naive \PopMuANaive, corrected \PopMuAHeckMB, truth \PopTrueMuA) but now
\emph{overshoots}, more so at higher noise. The robust practical estimator
is the boring one: refit the prior on \emph{all} observed prefixes,
including those of killed configs (\PopMuAEBAll, near-unbiased) --- exactly
the data a replaying tool already holds, reinforcing the crux that the fix
is to use the prefixes one has, not to model survivor selection. The same
posterior machinery cuts survivor-extrapolation bias at the first rung
from \ExtrapBiasNaive{} (naive least squares) to \ExtrapBiasHeckMB{};
a per-curve Tobit correction without the population prior manages only
\ExtrapBiasTobit{}, and an Arellano--Bond-flavoured lag-instrument
extrapolator is worse than the naive fit and is dropped. On LCBench and
PD1 replays the story is honestly mixed and matches the calibrated-noise
prediction: plain last-value SH is already near-optimal at decision
level; naive power-law extrapolation \emph{hurts} promotions and suffers
a winner's curse when picking from extrapolations
(top-decile prediction bias \TopBiasLCBPow{} on LCBench); the corrected
surrogate slashes that tail bias ($\sim$7$\times$, to \TopBiasLCBEB{}) and
ties last-value SH on LCBench decisions, but on PD1 it improves prediction
while \emph{worsening} decisions, and a one-parameter variance-penalty hack
captures most of the benefit for far less machinery --- a
prediction--decision gap we quantify rather than paper over.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Multi-fidelity hyperparameter optimization kills most of its training
runs early: successive halving \citep{jamieson2016}, Hyperband
\citep{li2018hyperband}, and ASHA \citep{li2020asha} advance only the top
$1/\eta$ of configurations at each rung. An active literature fits
\emph{learning-curve surrogates} to the resulting prefixes --- freeze-thaw
GPs \citep{swersky2014}, parametric extrapolation
\citep{domhan2015,klein2017}, deep-kernel multi-fidelity models
\citep{wistuba2022dyhpo}, power-law scaling fits \citep{kadra2023dpl},
and in-context curve extrapolators \citep{adriaensen2023lcpfn,
rakotoarison2024ifbo}. None of these models the sampler's censoring
mechanism, although every deployed curve the surrogate ever sees was
selected by it. This remains true in the newest work: a 2025 method that
predicts learning curves with latent-Kronecker GPs to \emph{reduce}
premature pruning \citep{lin2025lkgp} still trains its predictor on
survivor curves as if they were an unbiased sample, and a fresh
large-scale learning-curve database documents that curves are more
ill-behaved than assumed \citep{mohr2025lcdb} without connecting the
ill-behavedness to selection. The gap our pre-registered kill-test found
(\S\ref{sec:repro}) holds.

\paragraph{The honest crux.} Stated immediately, because it disarms the
obvious objection: SH selects on the \emph{observed} prefix, which the
surrogate also observes, so if prefixes were noiseless functions of the
latent curve the censoring would be ignorable --- missing at random,
nothing to correct. The bias enters through observation noise. A config
survives the rung at epoch $t_k$ if its \emph{noisy} value there clears
the bar; survival therefore selects on the noise realization
($\E[\epsilon_{t_k} \mid \text{survived}] = \GateEpsSurv$ at $\sigma =
0.02$, $\eta = 3$ in our controlled family), the fitted latent parameters
of survivors are noise-inflated, and their continuations disappoint the
extrapolation --- \citeauthor{galton1886}'s regression to the mean
\citep{galton1886}, in the form of a Tobit/Heckman selection structure
\citep{tobin1958,heckman1979} on the \emph{latent} curve parameters, with
Tweedie/empirical-Bayes shrinkage \citep{efron2011tweedie} as the natural
repair. This is a precise, testable claim, not a vibe, and it makes two
falsifiable predictions: the bias must grow with noise and with selection
pressure, and it must vanish as noise $\to 0$. Both hold
(\S\ref{sec:e0}).

\paragraph{Identification, up front (shared with the companion paper
\citealp{howe2026heckuq}).} Recovering the \emph{unselected} population
of curves from survivor-only data is a Heckman problem, and without an
exclusion restriction it rides on functional form. HPO has a natural
instrument: \emph{rung-assignment randomness}. Hyperband runs identical
populations under brackets with different rung schedules; bracket
assignment affects survival but not the latent curve. We show empirically
that the survival-corrected estimator is weakly identified from one
bracket and roughly halves the bias from three --- though it then
overshoots, so the estimator we actually recommend is the MAR one that
uses killed configs' prefixes (\S\ref{sec:e1}).

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Mechanism, dose-response, and an informative null}
(\S\ref{sec:e0}): in a controlled power-law family under SH, survivor
bias of naive extrapolation at the first rung grows from
\GateBiasSigLo{} to \GateBiasSigXhi{} accuracy units as $\sigma$ goes
$0.005 \to 0.04$ (and with $\eta$: \GateBiasEtaTwo{} $\to$
\GateBiasEtaFour); at LCBench's \emph{median} calibrated noise the bias
is $\approx$ zero. SH is robust exactly where its inputs are clean ---
and several real regimes are not (PD1 ImageNet: $\sigma \approx 0.02$).
\item \textbf{A corrected surrogate with an honest identification story}
(\S\ref{sec:e1}): a hierarchical Gaussian prior over pow3 parameters in
a Laplace/sufficient-statistic formulation with rung-survival probit
terms. Survivor-only + single bracket: weakly identified (we show the
likelihood prefers the biased solution). Survivor-only + three brackets:
bias roughly halved but overshooting (\PopMuAHeckMB{} vs truth
\PopTrueMuA{}, survivor-naive \PopMuANaive{}). The estimator we actually
recommend includes killed-curve prefixes (the data a replaying tool has
anyway) and is near-unbiased (MAR reference \PopMuAEBAll{}) --- the
theory's real payoff is not a survivor-only correction but the diagnosis
of \emph{which} data to fit on.
\item \textbf{Decision-level replays on LCBench and PD1}
(\S\ref{sec:e2}): naive power-law extrapolation \emph{hurts} SH
(regret \RegLCBPow{} vs \RegLCBLast{} for last-value on LCBench pools);
the corrected surrogate pulls it back to last-value parity there; the
winner's curse of picking from extrapolations is large (top-decile bias
\TopBiasLCBPow) and the corrected posterior cuts it $\sim$7$\times$
(\TopBiasLCBEB). The pre-registered null control --- a one-parameter
variance penalty --- captures most of that decision-level benefit for a
fraction of the machinery.
\item \textbf{The prediction--decision gap, reported} (\S\ref{sec:e2}):
the corrected surrogate improves predictions (RMSE, tail bias) everywhere,
yet does not beat plain last-value SH at matched budget --- and on PD1 it
is decisively \emph{worse} at decisions despite better predictions,
because refitting a prior on small pools adds decision noise the
prediction metrics miss. We say so plainly; it is the calibrated null of
\S\ref{sec:e0} made concrete.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Curve family.} Latent pow3 curves $y_i(t) = a_i - b_i
t^{-c_i}$, $t = 1..T{=}52$; $\phi_i = (a_i, \log b_i, \log c_i) \sim
N(\mu_{\text{pop}}, \Sigma_{\text{pop}})$ calibrated loosely to LCBench
validation-accuracy curves; observations $\hat y_{it} = y_i(t) + \sigma
\epsilon_{it}$, iid Gaussian. SH with rungs at $t \in \{4, 12, 36\}$,
keep-fraction $1/\eta$ by the observed value at the rung epoch.

\paragraph{Estimators.} \emph{Naive}: per-curve nonlinear least squares
(the standard extrapolating surrogate; \citealp{domhan2015,kadra2023dpl}).
\emph{Tobit}: per-curve truncation correction --- the survival events'
$\log\Phi$ terms added to the per-curve NLL (no population prior;
\citealp{tobin1958}). \emph{EB}: hierarchical Gaussian prior over $\phi$
fitted by marginal likelihood in a Laplace formulation (each curve
summarized by its LS fit $\hat\phi_i$ with observation covariance $V_i =
\sigma^2 (J^\top J)^{-1}$, so $\hat\phi_i \sim N(\mu, \Sigma + V_i)$ in
closed form); predictions are exact-likelihood posterior means via
importance sampling with the Laplace posterior as proposal (the Laplace
summary is only a proposal; its error cancels in the weights).
\emph{EB + Heckman}: the same, fitted on survivor curves only, with the
selection correction $-\,n\log P_{\psi}(\text{survive all rungs})$ ---
the joint selection+outcome likelihood; survival probabilities are
probit products over rungs, estimated by common-random-number Monte
Carlo over the prior. A first attempt that Monte-Carlo-estimated the
\emph{data} marginal from prior samples collapses for peaked likelihoods
(effective sample size $\approx$ 3 at $T = 52$, $\sigma = 0.01$); we
document this failure mode and use the closed-form marginal instead.

\section{B-E0: the gate --- bias exists, is dose-responsive, and
vanishes at clean-signal noise}
\label{sec:e0}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/fig1_mechanism}
\caption{(a) The mechanism: SH survivors (blue) at the first rung were
partly selected on upward noise; naive pow3 fits to their 4-epoch
prefixes (dashed) overshoot their true continuations (black). (b)
Survivor selection bias (survivor minus no-selection control) of naive
extrapolation at the first rung vs observation noise, $\eta = 3$, 8
seeds; green dashed lines: noise levels calibrated from real LCBench
curves (lag-1-difference median and pow3-residual median). (c) The bias
grows with selection pressure $\eta$ and decays across rungs as prefixes
pin the fits down.}
\label{fig:mechanism}
\end{figure}

For each rung we fit the naive surrogate on same-length prefixes of
\emph{all} configs (the no-selection control) and of survivors; the
\emph{selection bias} is the difference of their mean prediction errors,
isolating censoring from generic extrapolation error. Results
(Fig.~\ref{fig:mechanism}; $n = 1000$ configs, 8 seeds): at the first
rung ($t = 4$, where SH kills two thirds of the pool and the surrogate
extrapolates $13\times$) the selection bias is
\GateBiasSigLo{}$\pm$\GateBiasSigLoSD{} at $\sigma = 0.005$,
\GateBiasSigMed{} at $0.01$, \GateBiasSigHi{} at $0.02$, and
\GateBiasSigXhi{} at $0.04$ accuracy units --- monotone in $\sigma$, as
the mechanism demands --- and monotone in selection pressure
(\GateBiasEtaTwo{} $\to$ \GateBiasEtaThree{} $\to$ \GateBiasEtaFour{} at
$\sigma = 0.02$ for $\eta = 2, 3, 4$). By the third rung the bias is an
order of magnitude smaller (\GateBiasRungOne{} $\to$ \GateBiasRungThree{}
at $\sigma = 0.02$): long prefixes leave little room for noise-driven
selection to distort the fit. The pre-registered gate --- bias exists and
grows with noise and selection pressure --- \textbf{passes}.

\paragraph{The informative null.} Calibrating $\sigma$ from real LCBench
curves gives a median lag-1-difference noise of \CalibSigDiff{} (much of
LCBench's measurement error is a \emph{persistent} per-config validation-set
effect rather than per-epoch jitter) and a pow3-residual scale of
\CalibSigPow{}; at these levels the first-rung selection bias is
\GateBiasCalibDiff{} and \GateBiasCalibPow{} respectively ---
negligible to small. The honest conclusion, pre-registered as an
acceptable outcome: \emph{at LCBench-median noise, SH's censoring is
close to ignorable, and the practical bite of survivor bias is confined
to noisy regimes} --- small validation sets, quantized metrics, or tasks
like PD1's ImageNet ($\sigma \approx 0.02$ by the same calibration),
which sits squarely in the dose-responsive zone.

\section{B-E1: the corrected surrogate and its identification}
\label{sec:e1}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/fig2_correction}
\caption{$\sigma = 0.01$, 8 seeds, mean$\pm$sd. (a) Recovering the
unselected population mean $\mu_a$ from survivor-only data: the naive
survivor mean and uncorrected EB prior are biased upward; the
survival-corrected fit from ONE bracket is weakly identified (high
variance); THREE Hyperband-style brackets (rung-assignment randomness as
exclusion restriction) roughly halve the bias but overshoot downward; the
all-prefixes EB reference (using killed configs' prefixes) is nearly
unbiased and is the estimator we recommend. (b) Survivor extrapolation
bias by rung.}
\label{fig:correction}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Population recovery.} Fitting the extrapolator's prior on
completed (survivor) runs is common practice; it estimates the
\emph{survivor} population. At $\sigma = 0.01$, three brackets of 999
configs leave \PopNSurv{} completed curves whose naive mean asymptote is
\PopMuANaive{} against a true \PopTrueMuA{} (uncorrected EB prior:
\PopMuAEBSurv{}), a $+0.084$ upward bias. Adding the survival-likelihood
correction needs cross-bracket variation to bite at all: one bracket gives
\PopMuAHeckOneBr{}$\pm$\PopMuAHeckOneBrSD{} (weakly identified --- the
conditional likelihood \emph{prefers} the biased solution, which we
verified by evaluating it at truth vs.\ at the naive fit and finding the
naive fit scores lower). Three brackets give
\PopMuAHeckMB{}$\pm$\PopMuAHeckMBSD{}, cutting $|$bias$|$ from $0.084$ to
$0.036$ --- but now overshooting \emph{below} truth, and increasingly so
with noise ($0.76, 0.74, 0.66$ at $\sigma = 0.005, 0.01, 0.02$): the
functional-form correction is itself sensitive to the assumed noise model,
the classic Heckman fragility \citep{puhani2000}. The population sd is
recovered well (\PopSdAHeckMB{} vs true \PopTrueSdA{}). The estimator we
actually recommend is the MAR-correct one that refits the prior on
\emph{all} observed prefixes, killed configs included: it lands at
\PopMuAEBAll{}, essentially unbiased, and it is exactly the data a
replaying tool already holds. The parallel with the companion paper's
instrument-absent honesty curve \citep{howe2026heckuq} is exact --- same
machinery, same fragility --- but the practical lesson here is sharper:
because selection is on observed prefixes, one need not model it at all;
one need only fit on the prefixes rather than on the survivors.

\paragraph{Survivor extrapolation.} At the first rung the naive fit's
bias is \ExtrapBiasNaive{}$\pm$\ExtrapBiasNaiveSD{}; the per-curve Tobit
correction (no prior) shaves it to \ExtrapBiasTobit{} --- the truncation
terms only touch the marginal survivors --- while the exact posterior
under the corrected prior essentially removes it (\ExtrapBiasHeckMB{};
RMSE \ExtrapRMSEHeckMB{} vs \ExtrapRMSENaive{} naive). Since survival is
a deterministic function of the observed prefix, the \emph{posterior}
needs no extra selection term --- exact Bayes has zero conditional bias
given any data-measurable selection event; what selection breaks is the
\emph{prior} estimated from survivors, and that is where the correction
belongs. This decomposition is, to our knowledge, not stated in the
LC-surrogate literature.

\paragraph{Arellano--Bond variant.} The lag-instrument dynamic-panel
idea \citep{arellano1991} --- extrapolating increments with second-lag
instruments --- gives first-rung bias \ExtrapBiasABIV{}, worse than
naive least squares (power-law curvature defeats short-lag AR
structure). Per the pre-registered plan we drop it without ceremony.

\paragraph{Fresh-config transfer.} Priors learned from survivor-only
data and used on \emph{new} unselected configs (the ``surrogate trained
on completed runs'' deployment) show the same ordering but a small effect
at the $12$-epoch prefix we test, where even the naive fit extrapolates
well: bias \FreshBiasEBSurv{} uncorrected vs \FreshBiasHeckMB{} corrected
(\FreshBiasEBAll{} for the MAR reference). The transfer penalty of a
survivor-fit prior is real but modest once prefixes are moderately long.

\section{B-E2--E4: LCBench and PD1 replays}
\label{sec:e2}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figs/fig3_lcbench}
\caption{Replays on real curves (six LCBench datasets, four PD1 tasks;
8 seeds $\times$ pool sizes 50/200). (a) Rank fidelity of final-value
predictions from first-rung prefixes. (b) The winner's curse: mean
prediction error in the top decile of each surrogate's own predictions.
(c) Decision-level regret under SH replay with surrogate-driven
promotion, and under picking the best \emph{predicted} config from all
prefixes including killed ones.}
\label{fig:lcbench}
\end{figure}

We replay synchronous SH (rungs at fixed fractions of each task's
horizon, $\eta = 3$) on precomputed curves --- six LCBench datasets
\citep{zimmer2021lcbench} and four PD1 tasks \citep{wang2022pd1} ---
with the surrogate driving both promotions and the final pick.
Surrogates: last-value (standard SH), naive pow3, pow3 with a
one-parameter variance penalty $\hat y - \kappa\,\mathrm{sd}$ (the
pre-registered B-E4 null control, $\kappa = 1$), and the EB-corrected
surrogate fitted on the fly from all prefixes observed so far in the
replay. FT-PFN \citep{rakotoarison2024ifbo} is cite-compared rather than
run: its synthetic-prior pretraining mitigates per-curve overfitting ---
a shrinkage effect our EB model makes explicit --- but does not model
the deployed sampler's selection process, which is the object of study
here.

\paragraph{Predictions.} From first-rung prefixes, last-value ranks
configs surprisingly well (Spearman \SpearLCBLast{} on LCBench) but is
uniformly pessimistic (curves still rise); naive pow3 collapses the
ranking (\SpearLCBPow) and exhibits a large winner's curse --- the top
decile of its own predictions over-promises by \TopBiasLCBPow{} accuracy
units --- while the corrected surrogate recovers much of the rank fidelity
(\SpearLCBEB) with the best RMSE (\RMSELCBEB{} vs \RMSELCBPow{} naive)
and a $\sim$7$\times$ smaller tail bias (\TopBiasLCBEB{} vs
\TopBiasLCBPow{}). PD1 mirrors the prediction pattern (Spearman
\SpearPDLast{} / \SpearPDPow{} / \SpearPDEB; top-decile bias \TopBiasPDPow{}
naive vs \TopBiasPDEB{} corrected).

\paragraph{Decisions.} Here the story splits by benchmark, and we report
the split. Under promotion-by-surrogate on LCBench, last-value SH is
already near-optimal (regret \RegLCBLast{}), consistent with the
\S\ref{sec:e0} null at LCBench noise: naive pow3 promotion is \emph{worse}
(\RegLCBPow; paired Wilcoxon vs corrected $p = \PRegNaiveVsEBLCB$), and the
corrected surrogate pulls extrapolation-driven promotion back to
last-value parity (\RegLCBEB; vs last-value $p = \PRegLastVsEBLCB$, not
significant). On PD1, however, the corrected surrogate improves
\emph{prediction} yet \emph{worsens decisions}: its promotion regret
(\RegPDEB) is significantly \emph{above} last-value's (\RegPDLast;
$p = \PRegLastVsEBPD$) and above naive pow3's (\RegPDPow) --- refitting a
prior on small, heterogeneous PD1 pools each rung adds decision noise the
prediction metrics do not see. This is the prediction--decision gap in its
starkest form, and it is exactly what the pre-registered kill condition
told us to watch for. When the final pick may choose \emph{any} config
from its predicted final --- where killed configs' extrapolations compete
--- naive's winner's curse costs real accuracy (regret \RegAllLCBPow{}; it
picks an early-killed config in \AllpickKilledFracPowLCB{} of LCBench
replays), and both the cheap variance penalty (\RegAllLCBPowVpen) and the
corrected surrogate (\RegAllLCBEB) roughly halve that damage --- but
neither reaches plain last-value's \RegAllLCBLast{}. That is the B-E4
verdict, stated plainly: \emph{the one-parameter variance penalty captures
most of the correction's decision-level benefit}, and \emph{neither
intervention beats plain last-value SH at matched budget on these
benchmarks}. The corrected surrogate's value on these families is confined
to (i) whenever extrapolating surrogates are used at all --- it makes them
safer --- and (ii) unbiased final-value prediction itself (reporting,
meta-learning, transfer), not raw SH
regret at clean-signal noise.

\section{What we do not claim}

We claim nothing beyond the tested benchmark families (LCBench tabular
MLP curves; four PD1 tasks) and the pow3 curve family; other curve
shapes (double-descent, cyclic schedules) violate the parametric prior.
We do not claim corrected surrogates improve end-to-end HPO regret at
LCBench-median noise --- they do not, and \S\ref{sec:e0} explains why
that is the expected outcome, not a failure of the theory. We do not
claim asynchronous ASHA results; replays are synchronous SH. The
rank-based rung thresholds are treated as fixed constants in the
survival likelihood (standard, but an approximation). We make no claim
about FT-PFN's internals beyond the cited design.

\section{Limitations}

Observation noise is modeled iid Gaussian per epoch; real LCBench noise
has a persistent per-config component (fixed validation set) that acts
like latent quality for within-run decisions and is invisible to our
lag-1 calibration --- our two calibration estimates bracket it. The
Laplace summary degrades for 3--4-point prefixes (we correct predictions
with exact-likelihood importance sampling, but the prior fit inherits
some coarseness). The multi-bracket estimator assumes bracket assignment
is random --- true in Hyperband by construction, but not in adaptive
schedulers. Survivor counts below $\sim$50 leave the survival-corrected
fit weakly identified even with brackets.

\section{Reproducibility}
\label{sec:repro}

All code, tests (including the SH harness, Tobit direction, and
multi-bracket recovery tests), committed result JSONs, and the
auto-generated \texttt{numbers.tex} (regenerate-and-diff passes at
submission) are in the repository; discipline inherited from
\citet{howe2026eventrice}. LCBench data \citep{zimmer2021lcbench} and
PD1 \citep{wang2022pd1} are public; converter scripts and calibration
JSONs are committed. Total compute: under three GPU-hours plus CPU
replays on a 16-core desktop. Archived release: DOI
