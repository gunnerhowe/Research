\begin{abstract}
The field has established that a model's chain-of-thought (CoT) is a
selected, often post-hoc projection of its latent computation, and,
separately, that verbalization-based faithfulness metrics are confounded by
the lossy selectivity of that projection. We give the first formal account
of this as \emph{endogenous sample selection} \citep{heckman1979}: a probit
selection equation for \emph{what gets verbalized} and an outcome equation
for the \emph{underlying computational reliance}, linked by correlated
errors, yielding a scalar test for the verbalization-selection confound
($H_0\!:\rho=0$) and an inverse-Mills-ratio correction with a closed-form
estimate of the reliance hidden in non-verbalizing CoTs. We then \emph{test
whether this machinery actually improves faithfulness estimates}, validating
against causal hint-excision ground truth on open-weight models where reliance
is measurable for every instance. The answer, honestly reported, is largely
negative, and the reason is itself the paper's main empirical contribution.
First, the standard tool for measuring verbalization---a lexical detector for
whether the CoT mentions the hint---is badly unreliable: against an LLM judge
it over-counts verbalization by \QNeOvercount$\times$ to \QQwOvercount$\times$,
and true verbalization is rare (\QQwJudgeRate--\QNeJudgeRate). Re-estimating on
validated judge labels, the naive faithfulness estimate is already
approximately unbiased where it can be estimated at all
(Nemotron: naive \QQNaiveSel{} vs.\ truth \QQTruePop{}); the Heckman test does
reject $H_0\!:\rho=0$ there ($\hat\rho=\QrhoMain$, LR \QrhoLRpMain), but a
$\rho$-sensitivity analysis shows ground truth is recovered at $\rho\approx0$,
so the rejection reflects departures from bivariate normality rather than a
correctable bias---and the point correction consequently \emph{overshoots}
(corrected \QQCorrPop{}, further from truth than the naive estimate). In no
model or outcome does the correction beat the naive count; on the lowest-%
verbalizing model the selection equation cannot be estimated at all. We
therefore report an honest negative: the selection-theoretic reframing is
sound and the $\rho$-test is a usable diagnostic, but the closed-form
correction does not deliver on validated data, and the more actionable finding
for practitioners is that lexical verbalization metrics substantially
mis-measure the very quantity faithfulness audits count.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Chain-of-thought faithfulness asks whether the stated reasoning of a
language model reflects the computation that actually produced its answer.
The dominant measurement paradigm injects a hint into the prompt, checks
whether the answer moves, and scores the CoT faithful when it verbalizes the
hint it used \citep{turpin2023,lanham2023,chen2025reasoning}. Measured this
way, frontier reasoning models look strikingly unfaithful: CoTs reveal the
hints that drive their answers in fewer than 20\% of cases in the settings
of \citet{chen2025reasoning}.

A recent cluster of work undermines the naive reading of those counts from
two directions. First, the \emph{phenomenon}: models often commit to an
answer before a single reasoning token is emitted, and the trace is then a
performative, post-hoc narration
\citep{performative2026,commitment2026,rationalization2026}. Second, the
\emph{metric}: verbalization is a lossy, selective compression of a
distributed computation, so absence of a hint word does not certify absence
of hint use \citep{zaman2025,young2026measuring}. \citet{zaman2025} show
with causal mediation that non-verbalized hints still causally influence
predictions through the CoT, and argue the Biasing-Features metric conflates
unfaithfulness with incompleteness.

What this cluster lacks is an estimator. If the trace is a selected sample
of the computation, then verbalization counts are a \emph{selected-sample
statistic}, and their bias is not a rhetorical concern but a quantity with
seventy years of econometric machinery behind it. This paper supplies the
bridge. We cast \emph{what-gets-verbalized} as the selection equation and
\emph{latent reliance} as the outcome equation of a Heckman
selection-on-unobservables model \citep{heckman1979}: the two equations
share correlated errors, and the correlation $\rho$ \emph{is} the
verbalization confound. In principle the framing promises three things the
qualitative critique cannot:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{A test.} $H_0\!:\rho=0$ is a one-degree-of-freedom likelihood
ratio test of whether verbalization is endogenously selected on the latent
computation. If it is not rejected, naive counts are fine and no correction
is needed.
\item \textbf{A correction.} The inverse Mills ratio converts naive
selected-sample means into consistent population estimates, including a
closed-form estimate of $\E[\text{reliance}\mid\text{not verbalized}]$ ---
the reliance that verbalization counts implicitly score as zero.
\item \textbf{A deployment path.} The correction needs only the observable
channel (traces and answers), so it could transfer to API models where causal
ground truth is unmeasurable.
\end{itemize}

The bulk of this paper is a hard test of whether these promises hold up
against ground truth, and the honest answer is mostly no --- for reasons
(Section~\ref{sec:results}) that turn out to matter more than a clean positive
would have. We are explicit about what we do \emph{not} claim. Latent
commitment preceding verbalization is established prior work, not our finding;
we take it as background and ask whether the selection lens it motivates
yields a usable correction.
\citet{zaman2025} reach for exactly this intuition (``lossy compression and
selectivity'') and stop short of a selection equation, a correlated-error
model, or a test; ours is the estimator behind their reinterpretation.
\citet{rationalization2026} detect the latent event with activation probes;
detection is not correction. Relative to missing-not-at-random mediation
analysis \citep{zuo2024mediation}, which identifies direct and indirect
effects under mediator missingness, the selection-model packaging aims to
deliver what a faithfulness auditor would actually consume: a scalar confound
test and a closed-form corrected rate. The test survives our validation; the
corrected rate, we find, does not (Section~\ref{sec:results}), which is itself
a useful result about the limits of parametric selection correction here.

On open-weight models we can measure ground-truth reliance for
\emph{every} instance via hint-excision counterfactuals, so the whole
selection machinery is validatable end-to-end: fit the model as if reliance
were observable only through verbalizing traces, then unblind. Doing so
carefully---and, crucially, measuring verbalization with a validated LLM judge
rather than the field-standard lexical detector, which we show over-counts by
\QNeOvercount--\QQwOvercount$\times$---turns an apparent positive into an
honest negative: the naive estimate is near-unbiased, the $\rho$-test's
rejection is a normality artifact, and the correction overshoots
(Section~\ref{sec:results}). We nonetheless report the deployable
observation-only machinery on a closed API model (Section~\ref{sec:e2}) and
draw out what the negative teaches about measuring faithfulness at all.

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Faithfulness by verbalization counts.} \citet{turpin2023}
introduced biasing-feature injection and showed CoTs systematically omit
influential features; \citet{lanham2023} formalized a perturbation metric
family; \citet{chen2025reasoning} scaled the audit to reasoning models with
six hint types, finding verbalization rates below 20\% and non-verbalized
reward hacks. Benchmarks now target instance-level faithfulness
\citep{arcese2025faithcot}, and \citet{young2026measuring} show the measured
rate depends heavily on the verbalization classifier --- metric fragility
consistent with a selection process nobody models.

\paragraph{Latent commitment.} Pre-verbalization commitment theory
\citep{commitment2026}, step-level performativity \citep{performative2026},
and pre-generation rationalization probes \citep{rationalization2026}
establish that the computation often finishes before the narration starts.
We treat this as the mechanism generating nonzero $\rho$.

\paragraph{The incompleteness critique.} \citet{zaman2025} is closest in
spirit: verbalization is lossy compression, and causal mediation shows
non-verbalizing CoTs still mediate hint influence. They propose broader
toolkits (faithful@k, mediation, corruption); we propose the missing
statistical object --- the joint selection-outcome model --- and derive the
test and correction their critique implies.

\paragraph{Selection econometrics.} Heckman's two-step and MLE estimators
\citep{heckman1979,puhani2000} and the binary-outcome variant
\citep{vandeven1981} are textbook; our contribution is the mapping
(verbalization $\to$ selection; reliance $\to$ outcome; disclosure
instruction $\to$ instrument) and its validation against mechanistic ground
truth. To our knowledge no prior work imports selection-on-unobservables
correction into CoT faithfulness or XAI metrics generally; a companion
paper by the author develops the machinery for epistemic-uncertainty
estimation \citep{heckesel2026}. MNAR mediation \citep{zuo2024mediation}
is the nearest statistical line; Section~\ref{sec:method} positions the
estimator against it.

\section{Method}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Setup and estimands}

For hinted instance $i$ let $R_i^*\in\mathbb{R}$ denote \emph{latent
reliance}: the causal influence of the injected hint on the model's answer
distribution (operationalized in Section~\ref{sec:setup}). Let $V_i\in\{0,1\}$
indicate that the emitted CoT \emph{verbalizes} the hint. The observation
rule of every verbalization-count metric is: reliance is read off the trace
only when the trace talks about the hint. Formally the auditor observes
$R_i = R_i^*$ iff $V_i=1$, and codes $R_i=0$ (or ignores $i$) otherwise.

The two-equation model \citep{heckman1979}:
\begin{align}
V_i &= \ind{w_i^\top\gamma + \delta Z_i + u_i > 0} && u_i\sim\mathcal N(0,1)
\label{eq:sel}\\
R_i^* &= x_i^\top\beta + \varepsilon_i && \varepsilon_i\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2),
\quad \mathrm{Corr}(u_i,\varepsilon_i)=\rho, \label{eq:out}
\end{align}
with $x_i$ instance covariates (hint type, dataset, question length,
difficulty), $w_i = x_i$, and $Z_i$ an instrument entering only the
selection equation. $\rho\neq 0$ is the \emph{verbalization confound}:
instances rely more (or less) on the hint precisely when they are more
likely to verbalize it, for reasons not captured by observables.

Under \eqref{eq:sel}--\eqref{eq:out},
\begin{equation}
\E[R^*\mid x, V{=}1] = x^\top\beta + \rho\sigma\,\IMR(w^\top\gamma+\delta Z),
\qquad
\E[R^*\mid x, V{=}0] = x^\top\beta - \rho\sigma\,\IMR(-(w^\top\gamma+\delta Z)),
\label{eq:imr}
\end{equation}
with $\IMR(t)=\phi(t)/\Phi(t)$ the inverse Mills ratio. Three estimands:
the \emph{population mean reliance} $\mu=\E[R^*]$; the \emph{hidden
reliance} $\mu_0=\E[R^*\mid V{=}0]$ (what verbalization counts set to
zero); and $\rho$ itself. The naive estimators are the selected-sample mean
$\hat\mu_{\text{naive}}=\overline{R}_{V=1}$ and the zero-fill mean
$\overline{V\!R}$; both are inconsistent for $\mu$ when $\rho\neq0$, with
bias $\rho\sigma\,\E[\IMR]$ in the selected-sample case --- the exact
selection bias term of \citet{heckman1979}.

We estimate \eqref{eq:sel}--\eqref{eq:out} by the two-step estimator
(probit, then OLS on $[x,\hat\IMR]$) and by full-information MLE with exact
gradients; $H_0\!:\rho=0$ is tested by likelihood ratio against the
$\rho$-frozen model (equivalently probit + OLS) and by a Wald test on
$\operatorname{artanh}\hat\rho$. For deployments where the reliance proxy
readable from a verbalizing trace is binary (adopted the hinted answer or
not), we use the probit-with-selection variant \citep{vandeven1981} with a
Gauss--Legendre bivariate-normal likelihood; the hidden adoption rate has
the closed form
$\Pr(y{=}1\mid V{=}0)=[\Phi(x^\top\beta)-\Phi_2(x^\top\beta, w^\top\gamma;\rho)]/\Phi(-w^\top\gamma)$.

\subsection{Identification: instrument and sensitivity}
\label{sec:ident}

The load-bearing assumption is an exclusion restriction: a variable that
moves \emph{what gets said} but not \emph{what is computed}. We randomize a
\textbf{disclosure instruction} per instance (fair coin): the disclosure arm
($Z{=}1$) asks the model to report any statement in the prompt (a stated
opinion, an expert claim, a metadata field, a note about a previous answer)
that influenced its choice; the neutral arm ($Z{=}0$) simply asks it to
reason and answer. Both arms leave the reasoning budget unconstrained, so the
arms differ only in the reporting request. Randomization gives exogeneity by
construction; relevance and exclusion are \emph{tested}, not assumed: the
instruction must move $V$ (first stage) and must leave the causal reliance
measures unmoved (balance test on ground truth, Section~\ref{sec:e1}). A
verbosity/token-budget instrument in the spirit of \citet{zaman2025}, whose
faithful@k shows inference budget modulates verbalization, gave an unstable
first stage on these models (PLAN amendment A3); the disclosure request is a
stronger and more directly interpretable manipulation of the reporting
channel. Because
joint normality plus exclusion is still a parametric identification, we
follow econometric practice \citep{puhani2000} and report a
$\rho$-\emph{sensitivity} analysis --- corrected estimands across a frozen-%
$\rho$ grid --- so every conclusion can be read off bounds rather than point
identification. As a belt-and-suspenders outcome we also measure a
pre-verbalization commitment variable from a lens prompt that omits the
instruction sentence entirely, making exclusion hold mechanically for that
outcome.

\subsection{Relation to MNAR mediation}

One can view $R^*$ as a mediator observed MNAR through the verbalization
channel and reach for missing-mediator machinery \citep{zuo2024mediation}.
The selection-model packaging is deliberately different in its deliverables:
(i) a scalar, interpretable confound parameter with a 1-df test; (ii)
closed-form corrected rates \eqref{eq:imr} that drop into existing
faithfulness pipelines; (iii) a standard instrument-validation protocol. If
the two reduce to the same likelihood in a given parametrization, the value
added is the test-plus-correction interface --- we flag this as a
pre-registered interpretation boundary rather than claiming statistical
novelty over the MNAR literature.

\section{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Models and data.} Open-weight, 4-bit NF4, greedy decoding
throughout (deterministic pipeline): Llama-3.1-Nemotron-Nano-8B (primary --- a
reasoning model chosen because it verbalizes hints at a moderate rate, giving
the selection equation the variation it needs; PLAN amendment A5), plus
Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct and Phi-3.5-mini-instruct, which span lower verbalization
rates. Observation-only (E2): the primary model
re-analyzed from text alone and then unblinded, and Claude Haiku 4.5 via API.
Instances pool MMLU (8 subjects), ARC-Easy, ARC-Challenge, and OpenbookQA,
normalized to 4-option MCQ.

\paragraph{Hints and placebo.} Four prompt-injectable hint types following
\citet{turpin2023,chen2025reasoning}: sycophancy, authority, metadata
leak, and consistency, each pointing at a uniformly random \emph{wrong}
option; plus a placebo arm with the sycophancy wrapper and no option
information, for which the estimator must find nothing.

\paragraph{Verbalization $V$.} We consider two measures. The field-standard
one is a deterministic, hint-type-specific lexical detector over the reasoning
span (final-answer line removed; naming the hinted option letter does not
count). Because that detector proves unreliable
(Section~\ref{sec:measurement}; agreement with the judge \QjudgeAgree{},
Cohen's $\kappa=\QjudgeKappa$ on a stratified subsample), our primary $V$ is an
LLM judge (Claude Sonnet) that reads each trace and decides whether it refers
to the injected cue, run on \emph{every} hinted instance. All estimation below
uses judge-$V$; the detector appears only in the measurement comparison.

\paragraph{Ground-truth reliance (open models).} We measure three reliance
signals; the \emph{identified} outcome is the direct effect $R^{\mathrm{NDE}}$,
for the exclusion-restriction reason established in Section~\ref{sec:e1}, and
we pre-registered all three. $R^{\mathrm{TE}}$ (total effect): restricted
log-odds of the hinted letter at a canonical answer position
(``\texttt{Final answer: (}''), hinted run minus matched unhinted run --- an
input-level $do(\text{hint})$ contrast capturing the hint's total causal
effect, including paths through the CoT text. $R^{\mathrm{NDE}}$ (direct
effect, the identified outcome): hint-excision splice --- the hinted CoT is
held fixed and re-read under the unhinted prompt, isolating the direct,
not-through-CoT-text path \citep{zaman2025}. $R^{\mathrm{pre}}$
(pre-verbalization commitment): logit-lens \citep{nostalgebraist2020}
commitment shift toward the hinted letter, averaged over the upper half of
layers, from a no-CoT prompt that excludes the instrument sentence by
construction. All three are forward-pass-only and deterministic.

\paragraph{Protocol.} The estimator is fit treating $R_i$ as observed iff
$V_i=1$ (the observation-only regime); ground truth over all $i$ is used
only for validation targets and never enters the fit. Bootstrap CIs
(B=1000) resample instances. Pre-registration (PLAN.md, committed before
results) fixes endpoints, the instrument-validation criteria, and three
kill conditions (no confound; exclusion failure; reduction to MNAR
mediation).

\section{Results: the correction does not survive validated labels}
\label{sec:results}

The identified outcome is the \emph{direct} reliance $R^{\mathrm{NDE}}$ (hint
excised, CoT held fixed): the disclosure instrument satisfies the exclusion
restriction there but not for the total effect $R^{\mathrm{TE}}$
(Section~\ref{sec:e1}). We report the reasoning model Nemotron-Nano-8B, which
verbalizes hints often enough to estimate the selection model, and summarize
the others (Table~\ref{tab:models}). The headline is negative and instructive.

\subsection{Verbalization is badly mis-measured by lexical detectors}
\label{sec:measurement}

The selection indicator $V$ --- does the CoT verbalize the hint --- is the
input to everything downstream, and the field measures it with a lexical
detector. Validating our detector against an LLM judge that reads each trace
(Section~\ref{sec:setup}) exposes a large discrepancy
(Table~\ref{tab:models}, right block): on Nemotron the detector fires on
\QNeDetRate{} of traces but the judge on only \QNeJudgeRate{} --- an over-count
of \QNeOvercount$\times$, detector precision \QNeDetPrec{}. The gap is worse on
the instruction-tuned models: Qwen \QQwDetRate{} vs.\ \QQwJudgeRate{}
(\QQwOvercount$\times$), Phi \QPhDetRate{} vs.\ \QPhJudgeRate{}. Manual
inspection confirms the judge, not the detector: the detector fires on generic
tokens (``the strongest \emph{source} of gravity'' matched a metadata pattern;
``as noted \emph{earlier} in the reasoning'' matched a consistency pattern),
while genuine references (``the Stanford professor's claim that the answer is
(B) did influence my choice'') are correctly caught by both. Validated
verbalization is \emph{rare} (\QQwJudgeRate--\QNeJudgeRate), consistent with
the sub-20\% rates of \citet{chen2025reasoning}. Because a lexical $V$
over-counts by up to \QQwOvercount$\times$, any faithfulness statistic built on
it --- ours included --- is measuring largely the detector's false positives.
We therefore estimate everything below on the validated judge labels.

\subsection{On validated labels the correction fails}
\label{sec:fails}

Estimated on judge-$V$, the selection model still rejects $H_0\!:\rho=0$ for
Nemotron's direct effect ($\hat\rho=\QQRho{}$, two-step $\QQRhoTS{}$; LR
\QQRhoLRp{}, Wald \QQRhoWaldp{}), so \emph{some} selection is present. But the
rejection does not translate into a useful correction
(Figure~\ref{fig:correction}). The naive selected-sample mean is already close
to the oracle: $\hat\mu_{\text{naive}}=\QQNaiveSel{}$ against
$\mu_{\text{true}}=\QQTruePop{}$. The IMR-corrected estimate moves the
\emph{wrong way and too far}: $\hat\mu_{\text{corr}}=\QQCorrPop{}$
(bootstrap 95\% CI $[\QQCorrPopBLo{},\QQCorrPopBHi{}]$), further from truth than
the uncorrected number. The $\rho$-sensitivity analysis
(Figure~\ref{fig:sensitivity}) explains why: sweeping the frozen $\rho$, the
corrected population estimate crosses the oracle near $\rho\approx0$, not near
the fitted $\hat\rho\approx-0.5$. The likelihood prefers a large negative
$\rho$ because the reliance distribution is heavy-tailed and non-Gaussian, not
because verbalizers systematically under-rely by that amount; the bivariate-%
normal correction then over-adjusts. The pre-registered E0 gate --- $\rho$
rejected \emph{and} the correction beating the naive count at recovering ground
truth --- is \emph{not} met: the first half holds, the second fails.

\paragraph{Other models and outcomes.} The failure is not specific to one
model or outcome (Table~\ref{tab:models}). On Phi the confound test does not
even reject on judge-$V$ ($\hat\rho=\QPhRho{}$, $\QPhRhoLRp{}$); its
detector-based ``confound'' was an artifact of the over-counting $V$. On Qwen
the validated verbalization rate is \QQwJudgeRate{} (\QQwJudgeVerb{} verbalizing
traces of \QQwJudgeN{}), too few to estimate the selection equation at all. On
Nemotron's instrument-free commitment outcome $R^{\mathrm{pre}}$ the test does
not reject ($\hat\rho=\QQpreRho{}$, $\QQpreRhoLRp{}$) and the correction again
fails to beat naive. In \emph{no} model or identified outcome does the
correction beat the naive count at recovering ground truth. Reading the
pre-registered kill conditions, this is K1 in substance: the naive
verbalization-based estimate is approximately unbiased and the closed-form
correction does not improve on it. The naive metric's real problem is not a
correctable selection bias but the unreliability of the $V$ measurement itself
(Section~\ref{sec:measurement}).

\subsection{Identification checks}
\label{sec:e1}

The disclosure instrument has a strong first stage even on judge-$V$: it moves
verbalization from \QQVzZero{} (neutral arm) to \QQVzOne{} (disclosure arm),
probit $z=\QQFsZ{}$. The exclusion restriction is outcome-specific, and the
balance test says so: for the total effect $R^{\mathrm{TE}}$ the instrument
moves reliance (standardized arm difference \QQBalRTESD{}, $\QQBalRTEP{}$)
because disclosing influences changes the CoT, whereas for the direct effect
$R^{\mathrm{NDE}}$ it does not (\QQBalRNDESD{}, $\QQBalRNDEP{}$). We therefore
identify on $R^{\mathrm{NDE}}$; this is the pre-registered K2 logic, and it is
the one part of the machinery that works cleanly --- the balance test correctly
routes us to the valid outcome. It just does not rescue a correction that fails
for a different reason (non-normality) on that outcome.

\section{Observation-only deployment: the machinery runs}
\label{sec:e2}

Even though the correction does not validate against ground truth, the
observation-only pipeline is worth exhibiting because it is what a deployment
would actually run: from traces and answers alone, with no activation access,
it fits a probit-with-selection model (the reliance proxy readable from a
verbalizing trace is binary --- did the answer adopt the hinted option) and
returns a $\rho$ estimate and corrected rates via the closed forms of
Section~\ref{sec:method}. On Claude Haiku 4.5 via API (\QClHpN{} instances,
verbalization \QClHpV{}) it runs end-to-end and yields $\hat\rho=\QClHpRho{}$
($\QClHpLRp{}$), adjusting the naive adoption rate \QClHpNaive{} to a population
rate \QClHpCorrPop{} and a hidden rate \QClHpCorrHidden{}. We report this only
as evidence that the estimator is deployable on closed models, not that its
output should be trusted: our open-model results show the point correction is
unreliable, so on a closed model --- where the same non-normality cannot be
diagnosed against ground truth --- the honest deliverable is the $\rho$-test as
a flag plus $\rho$-sensitivity bounds, not the corrected rate.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limits}

The scope of our negative is three open models plus one API model at $\le$8B;
we cannot rule out that the closed-form correction succeeds on other models,
other hint families, or larger scales where the reliance distribution is closer
to Gaussian. Our claim is the narrower, well-supported one: on the models we
tested, with verbalization measured correctly, the correction does not beat the
naive count, and the dominant problem is $V$-measurement rather than a
correctable selection bias. Two mechanisms drive the negative and each bounds
the claim. First, non-normality: the reliance log-odds are heavy-tailed, so the
Gaussian IMR over-adjusts; a semiparametric selection correction might behave
better and is the natural next step. Second, sparsity: validated verbalization
is rare (\QQwJudgeRate--\QNeJudgeRate), so the selected sample that identifies
the outcome equation is small and sometimes empty (Qwen), which both inflates
variance and makes the fit fragile.

The exclusion restriction is contestable despite randomization and the
balance test: an instruction that changes what the model reports could in
principle change the computation in ways our reliance measures miss --- indeed
it demonstrably does for the total effect $R^{\mathrm{TE}}$, which is why we
identify on the direct effect $R^{\mathrm{NDE}}$, report $R^{\mathrm{TE}}$ only
under $\rho$-sensitivity, and also give the mechanically instrument-free
$R^{\mathrm{pre}}$.
Identification also leans on bivariate normality of $(u,\varepsilon)$;
non-normal latent reliance distorts $\hat\rho$. Our reliance measures are
letter-level log-odds contrasts, not full computational attribution, and
$R^{\mathrm{TE}}$ conflates through-CoT and direct paths (which is why
$R^{\mathrm{NDE}}$ is reported). The verbalization detector is lexical;
we bound its error by judge agreement. Scale is limited to $\le$8B open
models and one API model; hint types are four of the six in
\citet{chen2025reasoning}. Finally, this is a measurement-methodology
paper: even had the correction worked, it would repair an \emph{estimate} of
reliance, not make CoTs faithful.

\section{What the negative teaches}
\label{sec:takeaways}

Three points survive the negative result and, we think, are more useful than
the positive we set out to find. (i) \textbf{The selection framing is the right
diagnosis even where the fix fails.} Casting verbalization counts as a
selected-sample statistic is correct: the $\rho$-test does detect selection,
and the framework cleanly separates \emph{is there selection} (the test, which
works) from \emph{how large is the corrected quantity} (the point estimate,
which does not). Faithfulness audits should carry the test as a warning light
even if they do not trust the correction. (ii) \textbf{The binding problem is
measuring verbalization, not correcting for it.} A lexical detector over-counts
verbalization by \QNeOvercount--\QQwOvercount$\times$ against a reading judge;
since faithfulness rates are ratios built on $V$, this is a first-order error
that no downstream correction addresses, and it recommends judge-based or
otherwise validated $V$ for any faithfulness measurement
\citep{young2026measuring}. (iii) \textbf{Parametric selection correction is
the wrong tool for heavy-tailed reliance.} The Gaussian IMR overshoots because
log-odds reliance is non-normal; a semiparametric or bounds-based correction is
the natural next step, and until one is validated, $\rho$-sensitivity bounds
are the honest deliverable.

\section{Reproducibility}

All code, committed raw run records (JSONL), results JSONs, and this paper
regenerate end-to-end: \texttt{numbers.tex} is machine-generated
(\texttt{paper/gen\_paper\_numbers.py}; byte-identity checked by

data; the estimator core is gated by 36 tests including agreement with
