Companion work on algorithmic tasks argued that grokking's delay is the time to form task-structured representations: a contrastive prior controls whether and when generalization occurs, including ~17x delay-law-exponent flattening. We test what survives contact with natural data: MNIST grokking in the Omnigrok regime, with translated views as a label-free contrastive prior. The first result is negative and we lead with it: in a free-norm race the prior is slower on 5/5 seeds (median 1.31x). The auxiliary term freezes the weight norm at ~91-93 while the baseline's decays to 63-68 before generalizing -- the race confounds the prior's structure channel with its norm side-effect. A pre-registered amendment (predictions, kill criteria, and disclosures committed before any amended run) de-confounds it: at matched (pinned) norm the prior wins at every norm >= 50 on every seed, with criterion-level ratios 1.44-1.96x at norm 50 and 2.08-2.42x at norm 92, where the baseline fails the 0.85 criterion within the 100k-step budget on 3/4 seeds while the prior passes on 4/4 (at a 0.80 bar all cross; prior 1.32-1.84x faster). One pre-registered kill criterion fired: the exponent flattening does not transfer (slope ratio 1.17 censored lower bound, 1.03 refit at the fully uncensored 0.80 bar, vs ~17x on modular arithmetic) -- the prior buys a roughly constant multiplicative offset, not a slope change. Further arms: a cross-example nearest-neighbor prior is null at free norm (2-2, purity 0.81-0.84); the norm side-effect saturates by lambda=0.03, so no dose wins at free norm; the supervised prior transfers (2.06x, 5/5); wrong structure caps accuracy at 0.76-0.79; plain augmentation in the task loss abolishes the delay (17.9x) -- a regularizer effect, not representational priming. Upshot: free-norm comparisons systematically understate representational priors; evaluate them at matched norm.
