Emergent capabilities are widely treated as unpredictable: loss improves smoothly while abilities appear abruptly. Prior work offers early-warning indicators but never scores them as forecasts: no lead time at controlled false-alarm rate, no calibration, no negatives, no blind validation. We supply that discipline and show that, in grokking model systems and small language models, emergence timing is forecastable per run, in advance, with calibrated uncertainty. Across 30 transformers at identical configuration -- where the strongest published baseline predicts a single constant -- the formation time of the previous-token head forecasts each seed's induction-head emergence at Spearman rho=0.977 (CI [0.911, 0.995]) with median lead 975 steps (~15% of training). A best-case training-loss rule ties that ranking (rho=0.977) but with 50-step median lead: a nowcast. Conformal intervals 300 steps wide covered 15/15 held-out seeds (90% nominal). The entire rule was then frozen verbatim, commit-stamped, and subjected to a blind pre-registered gate on a never-seen configuration (wider model, sparser repetition): 10/10 interval coverage (bar 7/10), 10/10 pre-event alarms, 0/3 false alarms, with a ~configuration-invariant precursor-to-emergence gap (975 -> 1,012). All false-alarm claims are certified against 21 manufactured capability-blocked negatives (data-ablated and architectural). In public Pythia checkpoints the precursor leads the induction cliff in all 5 suites -- and all five cliff at the same token count, so public suites cannot test per-seed forecasting. The grokking half (466 runs) contributes the methodology and its lessons: a robustness-versus-false-alarm tradeoff, mechanism-factored gates that resolve it, calibration non-transfer, and a label-free probe that died under confirmation. Three kill criteria fired and are reported. Every freeze precedes its data in a public commit chain.
