\begin{abstract}
Transformer sequence models encode order through positional signals that are
ultimately tied to \emph{absolute} indices: sinusoidal and learned embeddings
address positions directly, and even relative schemes decay or rotate as a fixed
function of the index gap. Such signals saturate over long spans and generalize
poorly beyond the training length. We propose \textbf{Semantic Reference Frames
(SemRF)}, an attention bias that represents context and time \emph{relative to a
small set of learned semantic anchors} rather than to absolute positions. Each
token is softly assigned to its nearest anchor and encoded as a residual offset
within that frame; the attention bias between two positions combines an
anchor--frame affinity, a within--frame residual alignment, and a
\emph{frame--conditioned} temporal decay whose rate is a learned function of a
token's semantic frame. SemRF strictly generalizes ALiBi \citep{press2022train}
(recovered when the content terms are disabled and decay is frame--independent),
so it inherits ALiBi's length--extrapolation behaviour while adding
content--aware structure. The factorization is directly inspectable: trained on
enwik8 without any supervision, the anchors organize bytes into recognizable
character classes---lowercase, digits, uppercase, whitespace, punctuation,
markup---and the frames acquire markedly different learned time constants, with
structural tokens decaying slowest and word--internal letters fastest. The model
discovers, from data alone, that \emph{structure is long--range and orthography
is local}---a concrete realization of decoupling ``what'' (semantic frame) from
``when'' (relative time). This interpretable structure comes with strong
benchmarks: across three controlled diagnostics (associative recall, temporal
recency, selective copying) and character--level language modeling on enwik8, we
compare SemRF against seven positional schemes (NoPE, sinusoidal,
learned--absolute, RoPE, ALiBi, the T5 relative bias, and CABLE) under matched
backbones, parameter budgets, and training compute. \resAbstractFindings{}
Code and configurations to reproduce every figure and table are released.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Self--attention is permutation equivariant, so transformers require an explicit
mechanism to represent order. The dominant choices tie this signal, directly or
indirectly, to a token's \emph{absolute} position. Sinusoidal
\citep{vaswani2017attention} and learned \citep{radford2019language} embeddings
add a position--indexed vector to each token; rotary embeddings
\citep{su2021roformer} rotate queries and keys by an angle proportional to the
absolute index; and additive relative biases such as
T5 \citep{raffel2020exploring} and ALiBi \citep{press2022train} are fixed
functions of the index gap $i-j$. Two well--documented failure modes follow.
First, models trained at one context length degrade sharply when evaluated on
longer sequences---absolute encodings extrapolate worst, and even relative
schemes vary in how gracefully they decay \citep{press2022train,kazemnejad2023impact}.
Second, purely index--based signals conflate \emph{what} a token is with
\emph{when} it occurs, forcing a single monotonic decay to serve tokens whose
useful context ranges differ by orders of magnitude (a function word versus a
named entity or a numeral).

We ask whether order can instead be represented \emph{relative to stable
semantic reference points}---transferring to position and time the
anchor--relative representation principle that \citet{moschella2023relative}
introduced for latent--space communication, where coordinates relative to
anchors buy invariance to nuisance transformations of the underlying space. Our
method, \textbf{Semantic Reference Frames (SemRF)}, maintains a small set of
learned anchor vectors that partition the representation space into semantic
frames. Each token is softly assigned to its
nearest anchor and described by its residual offset within that frame. The
attention bias between positions $i$ and $j$ is then assembled from three
interpretable terms: (i) an \emph{anchor--frame affinity} that scores which
semantic frames should interact, (ii) a \emph{residual alignment} that refines
this within a frame, and (iii) a \emph{frame--conditioned temporal decay} whose
rate depends on the querying token's semantic frame. The third term is the key
departure from prior additive biases: ALiBi applies one slope per head to all
tokens, whereas SemRF lets the decay rate be a learned, per--frame quantity, so
that ``slow'' frames retain long--range information while ``fast'' frames stay
local.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
\begin{itemize}
  \item We introduce SemRF, a content--conditioned attention bias that encodes
  context and time relative to learned semantic anchors, and show it strictly
  generalizes ALiBi (Section~\ref{sec:method}).
  \item Under matched backbones and compute, we benchmark SemRF against seven
  positional schemes---including CABLE \citep{veisi2025cable}, the closest
  content--conditioned bias---on three controlled diagnostics and on enwik8
  character language modeling, with a length--extrapolation protocol throughout
  (Sections~\ref{sec:setup}--\ref{sec:results}).
  \item We ablate each SemRF component and the anchor count, and we analyze the
  learned anchors, showing they form interpretable semantic frames with distinct
  temporal decay rates (Sections~\ref{sec:ablation}--\ref{sec:analysis}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Absolute position.} The original transformer adds fixed sinusoidal
vectors \citep{vaswani2017attention}; GPT--style models learn them
\citep{radford2019language}. Both address positions by absolute index and
generalize poorly beyond the trained range.

\paragraph{Relative position.} \citet{shaw2018self} introduced relative position
representations; T5 \citep{raffel2020exploring} uses a learned, bucketed scalar
bias per head; Transformer--XL \citep{dai2019transformerxl} adds relative terms
to support recurrence. RoPE \citep{su2021roformer} encodes relative offsets
through rotations of queries and keys and is now standard in large models.

\paragraph{Additive biases and extrapolation.} ALiBi \citep{press2022train}
biases attention by $-m_h\,|i-j|$ with fixed per--head slopes and extrapolates to
longer contexts; KERPLE \citep{chi2022kerple} and Shortformer
\citep{press2021shortformer} pursue related goals, and randomized positional
encodings \citep{ruoss2023randomized} improve length generalization. SemRF's
temporal term is exactly ALiBi when the decay is frame--independent; we add
learned per--frame slopes and content terms.

\paragraph{No positional encoding.} Causal decoders retain some order
information even without explicit encodings \citep{haviv2022transformer}, and
NoPE can generalize competitively on some tasks \citep{kazemnejad2023impact}. We
include NoPE as a baseline.

\paragraph{Content--conditioned position.} A growing line of work makes the
positional signal a function of content. Contextual Position Encoding (CoPE)
\citep{golovneva2024contextual} gates which tokens increment a positional
counter; FIRE \citep{li2023functional} learns a functional map of relative
positions; and, closest to our temporal term, CABLE \citep{veisi2025cable}
learns per--token additive biases (scalar projections aggregated by cumulative
sums) that generalize ALiBi and improve extrapolation. SemRF differs from these
in \emph{how} content enters: rather than an unstructured per--token scalar, it
factorizes each token through a small learned anchor vocabulary---frame
membership, a within--frame residual, and a decay rate tied to the \emph{frame}
rather than the token. This structure is what yields the interpretability
results of Section~\ref{sec:analysis} (anchors align with character classes,
and frames acquire coherent time constants), which unstructured
content--conditioned biases do not expose. Because CABLE is the nearest prior
mechanism, we include it as an empirical baseline in all experiments
(Section~\ref{sec:results}). Our diagnostics build on associative
recall and induction \citep{olsson2022context,arora2023zoology} and selective
copying \citep{gu2023mamba}, standard probes of content--based long--range
routing.

\paragraph{Relative representations.} The principle of replacing absolute
coordinates with coordinates \emph{relative to a set of anchors} was
articulated by \citet{moschella2023relative} for latent--space communication:
representing samples by their similarities to anchor samples yields
representations invariant to isometries of the latent space, enabling zero--shot
model stitching. SemRF transfers this principle to a different object and
problem: we anchor \emph{positional and temporal} information in attention to
learned semantic reference points, seeking robustness to the absolute index
rather than to latent rotations. To our knowledge, anchor--relative
representation has not previously been used as a positional mechanism in
sequence models.

\section{Method: Semantic Reference Frames}
\label{sec:method}

\paragraph{Setup.} Let $x_i \in \mathbb{R}^{d}$ be the input embedding of the
token at position $i$ (no absolute positional signal is added). SemRF produces an
additive bias $b^{(h)}_{ij}$ that is inserted into the attention logits of head
$h$,
\begin{equation}
  A^{(h)}_{ij} \;=\; \frac{q_i^{(h)}\!\cdot k_j^{(h)}}{\sqrt{d_{\mathrm{head}}}}
  \;+\; b^{(h)}_{ij}, \qquad j \le i,
\end{equation}
and is computed \emph{once} from the token embeddings and shared across all
layers, exactly as ALiBi and the T5 bias are.

\paragraph{Anchors and residuals.} We keep $K$ learned anchor vectors
$A \in \mathbb{R}^{K \times d_a}$ (the semantic reference frames) and a
projection $W_u$. Each token is projected, softly assigned to anchors, and
described by its residual offset from the assigned anchor centroid:
\begin{align}
  u_i &= W_u x_i, &
  \alpha_i &= \mathrm{softmax}\!\big(u_i A^\top / \tau\big) \in \Delta^{K}, &
  r_i &= u_i - \alpha_i A .
\end{align}
A hard variant replaces $\alpha_i$ with a straight--through one--hot assignment.
Here $\alpha_i$ is the token's \emph{frame membership} and $r_i$ its coordinate
\emph{within} that frame.

\paragraph{Bias terms.} The bias combines three interpretable components:
\begin{align}
  \underbrace{\mathrm{sem}_{ij} = \alpha_i^\top B\, \alpha_j}_{\text{anchor--frame affinity}}, \quad
  \underbrace{\mathrm{res}_{ij} = \frac{(W_q r_i)\cdot(W_k r_j)}{\sqrt{d_r}}}_{\text{within--frame residual alignment}}, \quad
  \underbrace{\mathrm{time}^{(h)}_{ij} = -\,\sigma^{(h)}_i\,|i-j|}_{\text{frame--conditioned temporal decay}},
\end{align}
where $B \in \mathbb{R}^{K\times K}$ is a learned frame--interaction matrix and
the per--token, per--head decay rate is a convex combination of learned
per--frame slopes $s \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times K}$,
\begin{equation}
  \sigma^{(h)}_i \;=\; \sum_{k=1}^{K} \alpha_{ik}\,\mathrm{softplus}(s_{hk}) \;\ge\; 0 .
\end{equation}
The final bias is a gated sum
\begin{equation}
  b^{(h)}_{ij} \;=\; g_{\mathrm{sem}}\,\mathrm{sem}_{ij}
  \;+\; g_{\mathrm{res}}\,\mathrm{res}_{ij}
  \;+\; g_{\mathrm{time}}\,\mathrm{time}^{(h)}_{ij},
\end{equation}
with non--negative scalar gates $g_\bullet = \exp(\gamma_\bullet)$. By default the
content terms ($\mathrm{sem}, \mathrm{res}$) are shared across heads and the
temporal term is per--head.

\paragraph{Relation to ALiBi.} If $g_{\mathrm{sem}}=g_{\mathrm{res}}=0$ and every
frame shares a head's slope ($s_{hk}=s_h\,\forall k$), then
$b^{(h)}_{ij} = -\,\mathrm{softplus}(s_h)\,|i-j|$, which is ALiBi. SemRF therefore
\emph{strictly generalizes} ALiBi. We exploit this at initialization: we set the
per--frame slopes to ALiBi's per--head values and initialize the content gates
near zero ($g_{\mathrm{sem}}\!=\!g_{\mathrm{res}}\!\approx\!0.05$,
$g_{\mathrm{time}}\!=\!1$), so training \emph{begins} at a strong,
length--extrapolating operating point and learns semantic structure on top of it.

\paragraph{Cost.} SemRF adds $W_u$, $A$, $B$, $\{W_q,W_k\}$, and the slope table
$s$, totalling well under $\resSemrfOverhead{}$ of backbone parameters at the
scales we study. The bias is materialized once as a $B\!\times\!H\!\times\!T\!\times\!T$
tensor (like any additive--bias scheme) and reused across layers.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Backbone.} All methods share an identical pre--norm decoder--only
transformer \citep{vaswani2017attention,radford2019language} with GELU MLPs
\citep{hendrycks2016gaussian} and LayerNorm \citep{ba2016layer}; only the
positional mechanism changes. Synthetic tasks use a $d\!=\!256$, 4--layer,
8--head model---sized so that the content--matching (induction) circuit reliably
forms, which we verified in preliminary capacity probes consistent with the
recall--capacity effects reported by \citet{arora2023zoology}; enwik8 uses a
$d\!=\!384$, 6--layer, 6--head model ($\approx\!\resCharlmParams{}$ parameters).
We train with AdamW \citep{loshchilov2019decoupled}, a cosine schedule with
warmup, gradient clipping, and bf16 autocast, implemented in PyTorch
\citep{paszke2019pytorch} using its fused scaled--dot--product attention
\citep{dao2022flashattention}. All runs use a single NVIDIA RTX 3080 (10\,GB).
Full hyperparameters are in Appendix~\ref{app:hparams}.

\paragraph{Baselines.} We compare eight positional schemes: NoPE (none),
sinusoidal absolute, learned absolute, RoPE, ALiBi, the T5 relative bias,
CABLE \citep{veisi2025cable}, and SemRF (ours). Our CABLE implementation
follows the authors' reference code (per--layer $\mathrm{Linear}(d \to H)$
projections whose $-\mathrm{softplus}$ outputs are cumulatively summed, the
bias $S_i - S_j$ recomputed at each layer from that layer's pre--attention
hidden state, default initialization); see Appendix~\ref{app:hparams}.
Parameter counts per variant are reported alongside results.

\paragraph{Diagnostic tasks.} (1)~\textbf{Associative recall}: store $D$
key--value pairs, then answer queried keys; retrieval distance is recorded per
answer. (2)~\textbf{Temporal recency}: variables are repeatedly reassigned over
time and a query must return the \emph{most recent} value---an explicitly
time--sensitive task. (3)~\textbf{Selective copying}: reproduce sparse data
tokens, in order, from a field of blanks, probing position--robust routing. Each
scores only answer positions (a task--specific loss mask); all use fresh,
procedurally generated data.

\paragraph{Language modeling.} We model enwik8
\citep{hutter2006prize,mahoney2011text8} at the byte level (vocab 256) with the
standard 90M/5M/5M split, reporting test \emph{bits per character} (bpc).

\paragraph{Length extrapolation.} The central stress test: we train at one length
and evaluate at increasing lengths without retraining. Crucially, for the
diagnostics we grow the \emph{span} while holding the memory load fixed
(associative recall: the same number of pairs separated from the queries by a
growing filler gap, with the filler in--distribution at training time; temporal
recency: longer streams over the same variable set; selective copying: the same
number of data tokens in a growing blank field). This isolates \emph{positional}
generalization from recall capacity, which would otherwise confound the
comparison \citep{arora2023zoology}. For enwik8 we train at context $512$ and
evaluate bpc at $\{256,512,1024,2048,4096\}$.

\paragraph{Protocol.} Synthetic results use $\resSynthSeeds{}$ seeds and enwik8
uses $\resCharlmSeeds{}$ seeds; we report mean\,$\pm$\,std and, for the headline
extrapolation comparisons, paired significance tests across seeds
(Section~\ref{sec:results}).

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limits}

Our study is deliberately small--scale: a single consumer GPU, models up to
$\sim\!\resCharlmParams{}$ parameters, character--level LM on one corpus, and a
few seeds. These choices enable tightly controlled, matched--compute comparisons
but leave open how SemRF behaves at billion--parameter scale, on word/subword
tokenization, and against retrieval or state--space architectures. The
additive--bias schemes (ALiBi, T5, CABLE, SemRF) use PyTorch's math attention kernel
for arbitrary biases and are therefore somewhat slower per step than the
rotary/absolute schemes that use the fused kernel; we report throughput
alongside accuracy. Finally, SemRF introduces a small number of hyperparameters
(anchor count $K$, residual dimension, temperature) whose optima may be
task--dependent.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We presented Semantic Reference Frames, an attention bias that encodes context
and time relative to learned semantic anchors rather than absolute positions. By
construction SemRF generalizes ALiBi, and empirically it \resConclusionClaim{}
The learned anchors form interpretable semantic frames with distinct temporal
decay rates, offering a concrete mechanism for decoupling ``what'' from ``when''
in sequence models. We hope frame--relative position proves a useful primitive
for long--range and time--sensitive modeling.

\section{Associative recall in a harder regime}
\label{app:hard}
Before settling on the training gap distribution of Section~\ref{sec:setup}
(uniform in $[0,8]$), we ran the comparison with gap jitter up to 32
tokens. In that regime none of the schemes evaluated there escaped the
value-guessing plateau in any of three seeds within 15{,}000 steps (NoPE
$0.262$, learned $0.260$, RoPE $0.261$, T5 $0.284$, ALiBi $0.316$, sinusoidal
$0.174$; CABLE was added to the study after this regime was retired and was
not run here), while SemRF fully solved the training distribution in one of
three seeds ($1.000$; remaining seeds $0.33$ and $0.27$). We report this both as further evidence that
content-conditioned decay aids formation of the retrieval circuit, and as a
caution: near the formation phase boundary, small changes to the task
distribution move \emph{every} method's success rate, so comparisons must be
made in a regime where at least one method solves the task reliably. Full
per-run records are included with the released results
(\texttt{results/synthetic/assoc\_recall\_hard/}).
