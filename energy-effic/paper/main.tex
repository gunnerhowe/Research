\begin{abstract}
Delta networks spend compute only when an activation changes by more than a
threshold: each supra-threshold change is an \emph{event} that triggers the
corresponding weight-column MACs and memory traffic. A delta event is,
definitionally, a level crossing of the activation time series---yet the
delta-network literature tunes thresholds by grid search and only ever
\emph{measures} event counts after the fact. We connect this literature to
the century-old analytic theory of level crossings. \textbf{Predict:} on
three trained networks (GRU keyword spotting on Speech Commands, a
row-sequential-MNIST GRU, an enwik8 char-transformer), an empirical
crossing-rate profile fitted on a calibration split predicts per-channel
crossing rates on held-out data to \EmpMedianSCTwo--\EmpMedianEnwik{} median
relative error, and open-loop send-on-delta simulation on cached traces
predicts closed-loop delta-network event rates to within
\EventOpenloopMaxSCTwo{} (SC2) in the regime where the delta approximation
preserves accuracy. Rice's Gaussian formula works where activations are
near-Gaussian (LayerNorm-fed streams) and fails predictably with kurtosis
(GELU outputs, deep GRU states); an iid baseline fails by
\IidMinSCTwo--\IidMaxSCTwo. \textbf{Allocate:} inverting the predicted rate
curves turns a global event budget into per-stream thresholds analytically;
on Speech Commands this matches the Pareto front of a 48-configuration
random search at $\sim$2\% of its tuning cost, and realized event rates land
within \AllocRealizedErrSCTwo{} of target. \textbf{Train (negative):}
fine-tuning against a one-sided differentiable crossing budget makes traces
comply (crossings drop as demanded, increment scales shrink
\SigmaShrinkPsMNIST{}) but does \emph{not} improve the accuracy--events
Pareto front over post-hoc thresholding of the unmodified network---and
neither do L1-on-deltas or activity-rate controls. The mechanism is an
approximate scale-freeness: send-on-delta event rates depend on
$\theta/\sigma_\delta$, so smoothness training rescales dynamics without
restructuring them, while fixed-threshold comparisons misleadingly report
large savings (\AbsThetaSavingPsMNIST{} fewer events at matched absolute
$\theta$). Prediction and allocation work; training-for-temporal-sparsity,
in these tasks, does not beat the post-hoc baseline it is meant to replace.
We release estimators, receipts, and benchmarks.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Delta networks---delta-RNNs, sigma--delta networks, event-driven
transformers---spend compute only when an activation \emph{changes} by more
than a threshold $\theta$: each supra-threshold change transmits a delta,
triggers the corresponding weight-column MACs and memory traffic, and is
therefore a quantum of energy \citep{neil2017delta,oconnor2017sigmadelta,
yousefzadeh2022delta,gao2020edgedrnn,chen2024deltakws}. In this literature
the thresholds are tuned by post-hoc accuracy-vs-speedup sweeps and the
event counts are always \emph{measured after the fact}; no published
delta-network method predicts event rates from activation statistics or
trains against an analytic rate objective.

A delta event is, definitionally, a level crossing of the activation time
series. Level-crossing statistics have an analytic theory that is eighty
years old: Rice's formula \citep{rice1944,kac1943} gives the mean crossing
rate of a stationary Gaussian process at any level from two spectral
moments, and its smoothed Monte-Carlo estimator---built and validated as a
differentiable training objective in the preceding papers of this program
\citep{howe2026levelcrossing,howe2026budget,howe2026minkowski}---needs no
Gaussianity at all. This paper connects the two. It is the fourth paper of
the program, and the arc matters: \citet{howe2026budget} validated a
one-sided crossing \emph{budget} whose loss and gradient vanish within
budget, then reported honestly that PINNs fail by under- rather than
over-oscillating, so the budget was inert there. Delta networks are the
budget's native domain---excess crossings are excess energy \emph{by
construction}---and this paper is the test of that promise.

The results split cleanly into two positives and one negative, and we
report all three:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Validated estimator (E0).} The temporal segment estimator
(exact for piecewise-linear traces, differentiable, converging to hard
counts) and Rice predictions agree with ground truth to
$\le$\GateVRiceGP{} on synthetic processes with known spectra; the exact
discrete-time Gaussian crossing formula (an Owen's-$T$ expression) removes
the up-to-\OURiceFDWorst{} bias of the continuous formula on coarsely
sampled sequences. The send-on-delta event rate is bounded by total
variation over $\theta$; the gap---hysteresis of the anchor memory---is a
strikingly process-independent function $\gamma(\theta/\sigma_\delta)$ for
$\theta\lesssim\sigma_\delta/2$ (spread \GammaSpreadSmallX{} across four
process families).
\item \textbf{Prediction and allocation on real networks (E1--E2).} A
calibration-split crossing profile predicts held-out per-channel crossing
rates to \EmpMedianSCTwo--\EmpMedianEnwik{} median error across a GRU
keyword spotter, a row-sequential-MNIST GRU, and a char-transformer; Rice's
Gaussian form holds on near-Gaussian streams and fails predictably with
kurtosis. Open-loop simulation on cached traces predicts deployed
(closed-loop) event rates to a few percent in the regime where accuracy
survives. Inverting these curves allocates per-stream thresholds
analytically: on Speech Commands the resulting operating points match a
48-configuration random search at $\sim$2\% of its tuning cost.
\item \textbf{An honest negative with a mechanism (E3--E4).} Fine-tuning
against the one-sided crossing budget makes networks comply---crossing
profiles drop as demanded, increment scales shrink
\SigmaShrinkPsMNIST{}---but the accuracy--events Pareto front does
\emph{not} improve over post-hoc thresholding of the unmodified network.
Neither do the obvious alternatives (L1 on activation deltas, activity-rate
regularization, plain fine-tuning). The mechanism: send-on-delta event
rates are approximately scale-free---they depend on the threshold in units
of the increment scale, $\theta/\sigma_\delta$---so smoothness training
\emph{rescales} dynamics without \emph{restructuring} them. At matched
absolute threshold the budget-trained network fires
\AbsThetaSavingPsMNIST{} fewer events, which is exactly the comparison a
fixed-threshold evaluation would report as a win; at matched \emph{events}
the base network is as good or better. Post-hoc thresholding is a much
stronger baseline than the training literature assumes.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Delta and sigma--delta networks.}
\citet{neil2017delta} threshold changes of RNN inputs and hidden states
against an anchor memory of the last transmitted value and accumulate
matrix--vector products incrementally; the mechanism transfers to embedded
video \citep{yousefzadeh2022delta} and to silicon
\citep{gao2020edgedrnn,chen2024deltakws}, and underlies sigma--delta
quantized networks \citep{oconnor2017sigmadelta}. Thresholds are set by
accuracy-vs-speedup sweeps throughout. EGRU \citep{subramoney2023egru}
trains activity-sparse GRUs with surrogate gradients through a hard
threshold---spike-style training, not an analytic rate model.

\paragraph{Spiking transformers and spatial activation sparsity.}
Spikformer \citep{zhou2023spikformer} and SpikeGPT \citep{zhu2023spikegpt}
use spike \emph{coding}, a different mechanism from delta thresholding; we
cite and do not claim their ground. The activation-sparsity line
\citep{li2023lazy} concerns \emph{spatial} sparsity (which units are zero);
ours is \emph{temporal} sparsity (which units change).

\paragraph{Level-crossing theory in ML and signal processing.}
Rice's formula appears in machine learning in loss-landscape critical-point
counting \citep{bray2007,choromanska2015} and in our own program
\citep{howe2026levelcrossing,howe2026budget,howe2026minkowski}---never as an
activation-event or energy model. Zero-crossing statistics are classical
discrimination features \citep{kedem1986,kedem1994book}; send-on-delta
sampling theory \citep{miskowicz2006} bounds event rates by total
variation. The novelty here, as in the companion papers, is the
\emph{instantiation}: crossing rates as a differentiable model of, and
budget on, delta-network events.

\section{Theory: delta events as level crossings}
\label{sec:theory}

\paragraph{Setting.}
Let $a_c(t)$, $t=1,\dots,T$ be the sampled activation trace of channel $c$
feeding a delta-encoded matrix--vector product. The send-on-delta encoder
\citep{neil2017delta,miskowicz2006} keeps an anchor $\hat a$ (the last
transmitted value), fires when $|a_c(t)-\hat a|>\theta$, and updates the
anchor to $a_c(t)$ (anchor-on-event, preventing drift). Every event costs
the fan-out of the weight column in MACs plus the associated memory traffic
(Section~\ref{sec:setup}).

\paragraph{Rice's formula and its discrete-time form.}
For a stationary process with spectral moments
$\lambda_0=\operatorname{Var} a$ and $\lambda_2=\operatorname{Var}\dot a$,
the mean rate of up- and down-crossings of level $u$ is, under Gaussianity
\citep{rice1944},
\begin{equation}
\mu(u) \;=\; \frac{1}{\pi}\sqrt{\frac{\lambda_2}{\lambda_0}}
\exp\!\Big({-\frac{(u-m)^2}{2\lambda_0}}\Big).
\label{eq:rice}
\end{equation}
Sampled traces are sequences, not continuous paths: for a stationary
Gaussian \emph{sequence} with lag-1 autocorrelation $\rho_1$ the exact
per-step crossing probability has the closed form
\begin{equation}
P\big[(a_t-u)(a_{t+1}-u)<0\big] \;=\; 4\,
\mathrm{T}\!\Big(\tfrac{u-m}{\sqrt{\lambda_0}},
\sqrt{\tfrac{1-\rho_1}{1+\rho_1}}\Big),
\label{eq:discrete}
\end{equation}
with $\mathrm{T}$ Owen's T function \citep{owen1956}; at $u=m$ this is the
classical $\arccos(\rho_1)/\pi$ \citep{kedem1994book}, and $\rho_1\to 0$
recovers the iid value $2\Phi(\tilde u)(1-\Phi(\tilde u))$.
Eq.~\eqref{eq:rice} with finite-difference moments underpredicts
Eq.~\eqref{eq:discrete} as correlation weakens---by up to \OURiceFDWorst{}
at $\rho_1=0.1$ (Section~\ref{sec:e0})---so we use \eqref{eq:rice} as the
headline Gaussian predictor, as the delta literature would, and
\eqref{eq:discrete} as the refined form (both are reported).

\paragraph{Differentiable estimator.}
The program's smoothed Monte-Carlo estimator \citep{howe2026levelcrossing}
evaluates the co-area integrand $\delta_\epsilon(a-u)\,|\dot a|$ at sample
points. On a sampled trace, the piecewise-linear segment
$a_t\!\to\!a_{t+1}$ admits an \emph{exact} smoothed count,
\begin{equation}
\hat c(u) \;=\; \frac{1}{(T-1)\,\Delta t}\sum_{t=1}^{T-1}
\Big|\,\Phi\big(\tfrac{a_{t+1}-u}{\epsilon}\big)-
\Phi\big(\tfrac{a_{t}-u}{\epsilon}\big)\Big|,
\label{eq:segment}
\end{equation}
which converges to the hard sign-change count as $\epsilon\to0$, remains
differentiable in the trace values---hence in the network parameters---and
is robust to steps that jump many $\epsilon$ in one $\Delta t$, where the
midpoint co-area rule undercounts (Fig.~\ref{fig:e0}b,c).

\paragraph{From crossings to delta events.}
Summing crossing rates over the implicit ladder of transmission levels
spaced $\theta$ apart bounds the send-on-delta event rate by total
variation \citep{miskowicz2006}:
\begin{equation}
R(\theta)\;\le\;\min\!\big(\E|\Delta a|/(\theta\,\Delta t),\;1/\Delta t\big),
\qquad \E|\dot a| = \sqrt{2\lambda_2/\pi}\ \text{(Gaussian)}.
\label{eq:bound}
\end{equation}
The bound is loose by exactly the hysteresis of the anchor (re-crossing a
level without traveling $\theta$) and by multi-level jumps. We measure this
gap once, on synthetic processes, as a correction
$\gamma(\theta/\sigma_\delta)$ with $\sigma_\delta$ the one-step increment
scale (Fig.~\ref{fig:gamma}); for $\theta\le\sigma_\delta/2$ it is
process-independent to \GammaSpreadSmallX{} across four process families.
On real activation traces, whose increments are heavy-tailed, the
synthetic $\gamma$ transfers imperfectly (up to
\AnalyticGammaWorstSCTwo{} error; Section~\ref{sec:e1}); the empirical
route---simulating the encoder on cached calibration traces, which costs no
extra network forwards---is what allocation uses.

\paragraph{One-sided crossing budget.}
The training objective transplants the \citet{howe2026budget} mechanism to
its native domain: per layer $\ell$ with hidden traces $h_\ell$,
\begin{equation}
\loss_{\mathrm{budget}}^{(\ell)} \;=\;
\operatorname{mean}_j\;
\frac{\mathrm{relu}\big(\hat c_{\ell}(u_j) - b_j\big)^2}
{(b_j + \overline{b})^2},
\qquad b_j = \rho\, c^{\mathrm{cal}}_{\ell}(u_j),
\label{eq:budget}
\end{equation}
with levels $u_j$ on a quantile ladder of the layer's calibration
activation distribution (the dense-ladder rule of
\citet{howe2026minkowski}), budgets a $\rho$-scaled copy of the calibration
profile, and $\hat c$ the segment estimator \eqref{eq:segment}. Within
budget the loss and its gradient are exactly zero (one-sidedness;
unit-tested), so compliant dynamics are untouched. The sampling-scale
failure mode of \citet{howe2026minkowski} does not arise here: traces are
densely sampled 1-D sequences.

\section{Experimental setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\paragraph{Tasks and models.}
(i) \textbf{SC2}: Google Speech Commands~v2 \citep{warden2018speech},
35-class keyword spotting, 40-bin log-mel features (25\,ms window, 10\,ms
hop, $T{=}101$), 2-layer GRU \citep{cho2014gru} with 128 units, mean-pool,
linear head; test accuracy \BaseAccSCTwo{}$\pm$\BaseAccSdSCTwo{} over 8
seeds. (ii) \textbf{psMNIST}: row-sequential MNIST \citep{lecun1998mnist}
($T{=}28$ rows of 28 pixels), same GRU;
\BaseAccPsMNIST{}$\pm$\BaseAccSdPsMNIST{} over 3 seeds. (iii)
\textbf{enwik8} (secondary): byte-level char-LM \citep{mahoney2011enwik8}
on a 20M-character slice, 2-layer causal transformer (dim 128, 4 heads, FFN
512, $T{=}256$), \BaseBpcEnwik{} test bpc over 3 seeds. The delta-GRU
follows \citet{neil2017delta} exactly (thresholds on input and hidden
streams, anchor-on-event, incremental accumulators); at $\theta{=}0$ it
reproduces \texttt{torch.nn.GRU} to numerical precision (unit-tested). For
the transformer, the input streams of the QKV, attention-output, and both
FFN projections are delta-encoded along the sequence axis; attention
score/value matmuls stay dense, as in the event-driven transformer
literature. All experiments run on one RTX~3080 (10\,GB).

\paragraph{Events and modeled energy.}
One event = one transmitted vector component = its weight-column fan-out in
MACs plus the associated SRAM traffic. We report MAC-weighted event rates
as fractions of the dense network, and modeled energy using
\citet{horowitz2014} 45\,nm figures (FP32 MAC 4.6\,pJ, 32-bit SRAM read
5\,pJ, weights SRAM-resident). \emph{The paper reports event counts and
modeled energy, never measured watts.}

\paragraph{Discipline.}
Every number in this paper is machine-generated from committed result
JSONs (\texttt{gen\_paper\_numbers.py}, \texttt{make\_figures.py}); the
regenerate-and-diff check passes at submission. All estimator and
delta-cell properties are unit-tested (\texttt{pytest}, 14 tests). Gates
were pre-registered in the project brief: GATE~V (estimator vs closed
forms, $\le$3\%), GATE~P (calibration-split predictor within $\sim$10\% on
real networks), and the E4 kill condition (budget $\approx$ L1-delta at
matched events must be reported as such).

\paragraph{Event rates.}
Deployed (closed-loop) event rates are predicted from base-network
statistics by open-loop send-on-delta simulation on the cached calibration
traces: max error \EventOpenloopMaxSCTwo{} (SC2) and
\EventOpenloopMaxPsMNIST{} (psMNIST) across all streams and thresholds in
the regime where accuracy stays within a point of dense; prediction
degrades only where the delta approximation itself breaks the network
(psMNIST $x\ge1$, accuracy $<$52\%), i.e.\ outside any useful operating
point (Fig.~\ref{fig:events}). The fully analytic route (Gaussian TV +
synthetic $\gamma$) misses by up to \AnalyticGammaWorstSCTwo{} on real
traces---their increments are heavy-tailed, violating the small-$x$
universality of Fig.~\ref{fig:gamma}---which is why allocation uses the
empirical curves. On enwik8, thresholding costs quality quickly
(\BaseBpcEnwik$\to$\BpcAtXPointOne{} bpc already at $x{=}0.1$,
\EventsAtXPointOneEnwik{} events): character streams carry little temporal
redundancy at the feature level, unlike audio or image rows; prediction
still works (max \EventOpenloopMaxEnwikDeploy{} error at $x\le0.1$), but
delta deployment is not attractive there, consistent with event-driven
transformers targeting video/event streams rather than text.

\section{E2: analytic threshold allocation}
\label{sec:e2}

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figs/e2_pareto.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Analytic allocation vs threshold search} (test accuracy
vs MAC-weighted events, mean$\pm$sd over 3 seeds). The analytic operating
points (one calibration pass, zero search) lie on the random-search front
in the regime where accuracy is preserved; random search needs
$\sim$48 configurations ($48\times$ the evaluation cost) to match them.}
\label{fig:e2}
\end{figure}

Deployment requires per-stream thresholds $(\theta_{\mathrm{in}},
\theta_{h_1}, \theta_{h_2})$. The incumbent tunes them by search, each
configuration costing a validation evaluation. We instead record traces in
\emph{one} calibration pass, build per-stream rate curves $R_s(\theta)$ by
open-loop simulation on the cached traces (no further network forwards),
and invert them for a target budget: a single relative threshold $x^*$
(with $\theta_s = x^*\sigma_{\delta,s}$) solved by bisection on the
predicted aggregate curve; a proportional-share variant; and a single
absolute $\theta$ (all use the predicted curves; they differ in how
thresholds distribute across streams).

Fig.~\ref{fig:e2}: on SC2 the realized event budgets land within
\AllocRealizedErrSCTwo{} of target for budgets $\ge$20\% of dense, and the
analytic points sit on the 48-configuration random-search front. At the
tightest budget that costs $\le$1 accuracy point, the analytic allocation
needs \FracAtOnePtAnalyticSCTwo{} of dense events vs
\FracAtOnePtRSFortyEightSCTwo{} for the full 48-configuration search and
\FracAtOnePtRSSixSCTwo{} for a 6-configuration search---i.e.\ it matches
the asymptotic sweep and dominates the cost-matched one at
\TuneCostRatioSCTwo{} lower tuning cost. On psMNIST the same holds at
moderate budgets, while at aggressive budgets the joint search exploits
per-stream asymmetries the analytic heuristics do not
(\FracAtOnePtAnalyticPsMNIST{} vs \FracAtOnePtRSFortyEightPsMNIST); the
allocation-rule choice among our three variants is second-order
everywhere---what replaces the sweep is the predicted curves themselves.
In the accuracy-collapse regime, realized events undershoot targets
(feedback reduces activity; consistent with Fig.~\ref{fig:events});
allocation there is meaningless anyway. As a modeled-energy anchor: at the
20\%-of-dense operating point, SC2 keyword spotting runs at
\AccAtTwentySCTwo{} accuracy for \EnergyDeltaPerDecisionUJ\,$\mu$J per
decision vs \EnergyDensePerDecisionUJ\,$\mu$J dense
(\EnergyRatioAtTwenty{} modeled reduction; Horowitz-table energy, not
measured watts).

Per task and seed, the base model is fine-tuned (5 epochs) with

with the null controls: L1 on one-step deltas $|h_t-h_{t-1}|$, a
spiking-style activity penalty $\overline{h^2}$, a \emph{plain} fine-tune
(no regularizer; isolates the effect of extra epochs), and the post-hoc
sweep on the unmodified network. Every model is then evaluated closed-loop
over a threshold sweep $\theta_s = x\,\sigma_{\delta,s}$ using \emph{its
own} calibration statistics (thresholds re-tuned per model, as deployment
would), giving each model its own accuracy--events front.

\textbf{The budget does what it promises---and it does not help.}
Compliance is real: the budget loss falls to near zero, crossing profiles
drop as demanded, and hidden increment scales shrink
\SigmaShrinkPsMNIST{} at $\rho{=}0.2$ (psMNIST). But at matched events the
front never improves (Fig.~\ref{fig:e3}): at the reference budget of
\RefFracPsMNIST{} of dense events on psMNIST, post-hoc reaches
\E3PosthocPsMNIST{} vs the budget family's \E3BudgetPsMNIST{} (paired
difference \E3PairedDiffPsMNIST); on SC2 at \RefFracSCTwo{},
\E3PosthocSCTwo{} vs \E3BudgetSCTwo{} (paired difference
\E3PairedDiffSCTwo, $n{=}$\E3PairedNSCTwo{} seeds, Wilcoxon
$p{=}$\E3WilcoxonPSCTwo). The controls tie the post-hoc front
(plain \E3PlainPsMNIST{}, L1 \E3LOnePsMNIST{}, rate \E3RatePsMNIST{} on
psMNIST)---so the pre-registered kill condition fires in its strongest
form: not only does the crossing budget fail to beat L1-delta, \emph{no}
temporal-smoothness fine-tune beats doing nothing.

\textbf{Mechanism: approximate scale-freeness.} A send-on-delta encoder
driven at $\theta = x\,\sigma_\delta$ has event rate $\approx
\gamma(x)\,\mathrm{TV}/(x\,\sigma_\delta)$ with $\mathrm{TV}\propto
\sigma_\delta$: the scale cancels. Training that shrinks $\sigma_\delta$
therefore moves a model \emph{along} its own front, not above it; only
restructuring the increment distribution's \emph{shape} could help, and
Fig.~\ref{fig:e4} shows the budget compresses scale rather than shape. The
flip side is a warning about evaluation methodology: at matched
\emph{absolute} $\theta$ the budget-trained model fires
\AbsThetaSavingPsMNIST{} fewer events (at \AbsThetaCostPsMNIST{} accuracy
cost)---a fixed-threshold comparison, the natural default, would report
this as a large win. On the matched-events front the same model is
dominated by post-hoc thresholding of its own base. Fixed-$\theta$
comparisons overstate what smoothness training buys.

\paragraph{Overhead.} The budget costs \OverheadPct{} extra training time
(\TaskOnlyMs\,ms $\to$ \WithBudgetMs\,ms per step, batch 256;
segment-estimator forward+backward \EstimatorMs\,ms), consistent with the
$O(NL)$ estimator; the negative result is not a cost story.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\paragraph{What the theory buys.}
The validated chain is: activation statistics $\to$ crossing profiles
(Rice where Gaussian, empirical profile in general) $\to$ event rates
(open-loop simulation; TV bound with a universal small-$x$ hysteresis
correction on Gaussian-increment processes) $\to$ analytic threshold
allocation that replaces sweeps. This is the piece the delta-network
literature lacked: DeltaKWS-style deployments tune thresholds by silicon-
or simulator-in-the-loop sweeps; a calibration pass plus curve inversion
gets there for a few percent error at \TuneCostRatioSCTwo{} less tuning
compute on SC2.

\paragraph{What it does not buy.}
Training against the rate model (or any smoothness proxy) is
Pareto-neutral at best under scale-adapted thresholds. This is consistent
with, and explains, the delta literature's practice of training normally
and thresholding afterwards: our results say that practice is not lazy, it
is \emph{correct} for these architectures and tasks. The scale-freeness
argument suggests where a positive result might live: objectives that
restructure increment \emph{shape} (e.g., pushing traces toward
piecewise-constant, genuinely sparse-in-time increments) rather than
shrinking increment scale---EGRU-style hard gating \citep{subramoney2023egru}
is one such mechanism, and bridging it to analytic rate models is open.

\paragraph{Information per event (framing).}
One can read the event stream through Palm calculus: conditioning on event
times, the relevant quantity is information per event (bits per joule
under the energy model). Our negative says fine-tuning did not increase
bits-per-event at matched accuracy; we flag this as framing, not a
load-bearing claim.

\paragraph{Neuromorphic outlook.}
The prediction/allocation machinery is exactly what event-driven hardware
needs at design time: given trace statistics from a workload corpus, event
rates---hence energy and bandwidth envelopes---follow analytically, before
silicon or even simulation. The modeled \EnergyRatioAtTwenty{} energy
reduction at \AccAtTwentySCTwo{} accuracy for always-on keyword spotting is
the standard delta-network story \citep{chen2024deltakws}, now with
predicted rather than swept thresholds. We claim no measured silicon
numbers.

\section{What we do not claim}
We do not claim spiking or spike-coding results (Spikformer/SpikeGPT are a
different mechanism); we do not claim measured hardware energy (all energy
is Horowitz-table modeled, weights SRAM-resident); we do not claim the
training negative generalizes past the tested scale (GRUs to 128 units,
transformers to 2 layers, three tasks) or to training-from-scratch with
event costs in the loss from step zero; we do not claim the analytic
$\gamma$ transfers to heavy-tailed increments (it does not; measured at up
to \AnalyticGammaWorstSCTwo{} error); and the Palm-calculus paragraph is
framing only.

\section{Limitations}
Fine-tuning (5 epochs) rather than from-scratch training; one architecture
family per task; the budget acts on hidden-state streams via pooled
per-layer quantile ladders (per-channel budgets untested); the allocation
heuristics are one-dimensional inversions, not joint optimizers, and lose
to joint search at accuracy-destroying budgets on psMNIST; enwik8 quality
under thresholding is evaluated on fixed windows (no KV-cache-aware
decoding); event accounting charges each matvec's input stream
independently (a shared encoder would shave the anchor-update term); and
all energy numbers are model-based.

\section{Discrete-time Gaussian crossing rate}
\label{app:discrete}
For a stationary Gaussian sequence with mean $m$, variance $\lambda_0$,
lag-1 correlation $\rho_1$, the pair $(a_t, a_{t+1})$ is bivariate normal;
with $h=(u-m)/\sqrt{\lambda_0}$,
$P[a_t\le u, a_{t+1}\le u] = \Phi(h) - 2\mathrm{T}(h, a)$ with
$a=\sqrt{(1-\rho_1)/(1+\rho_1)}$ \citep{owen1956}, giving the crossing
probability $2[\Phi(h)-\Phi_2(h,h;\rho_1)] = 4\mathrm{T}(h,a)$
(Eq.~\ref{eq:discrete}). Sanity limits: $\rho_1\to0$ gives
$2\Phi(h)(1-\Phi(h))$ (iid); $u=m$ gives $\arccos(\rho_1)/\pi$; both are
unit-tested against AR(1) simulation (max error \GateVOU). On real traces
Eq.~\eqref{eq:discrete} tracks Eq.~\eqref{eq:rice} closely (both fail
together under non-Gaussianity): median errors differ by $<$2\% on all
streams, so the headline text reports \eqref{eq:rice}.
