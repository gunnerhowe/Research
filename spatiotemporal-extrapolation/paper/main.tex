\title{\vspace{-3em}Interpolation Beats Finite-Size Scaling for Domain\\
Extension of Learned Transfer-Operator Spectra:\\
A Two-System Study and a Convergence Diagnostic}
\author{Gunner Levi Howe\\ \small\texttt{gunnerlevihowe@gmail.com}}
\date{July 2026}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Can one predict the long-time statistics of an extensively chaotic field on a
large domain by learning a transfer operator only on small domains and
extrapolating its spectrum in system size? We built the finite-size-scaling (FSS)
machinery to do exactly this --- estimate leading Ruelle--Pollicott / Koopman
resonances and spectral densities by delay-embedded EDMD, fit a per-wavenumber
$1/L$ flow on small domains, extrapolate --- and the honest verdict, across two
systems chosen to bracket the finite-size convergence rate, is that it does
\emph{not} beat the simplest baseline: interpolating the spectrum of the largest
affordable box in wavenumber, with no size flow at all. For Kuramoto--Sivashinsky
(KS) the flow works in absolute terms --- $\{22,44,66,88\}\!\to\!L=1408$
($\TargetFactor\times$ the base volume, zero large-domain data) predicts decay
rates to \FlowTargetGamma, spectral density to \FlowTargetSPct, correlations to
\FlowTargetC --- but is \emph{redundant}: KS spectra converge by $L=88$ (drift
\ConvEightEightGamma), so interpolation does better (\InterpEightEightGamma). We
then ran the Nikolaevskiy equation, whose marginal $k{=}0$ conservation mode makes
the \emph{spectrum} converge slowly (drift \NikConvEightEight\ at $L=88$): here
interpolation is genuinely degraded (\NikInterpGamma\ on decay rates at
$L=\NikTargetL$) --- yet the $1/L$ flow is \emph{worse still} (\NikFlowGamma),
because the spectral density stays extensive and the flow overshoots the few
non-monotone, non-converged low-$k$ decay rates. The FSS flow has no advantage
window over interpolation in either regime. The operative diagnostic is not the
correlation length (short, $\approx\!\KSXiEightEight$ and $\NikXiEightEight$, for
both) but the \emph{small-domain spectral drift}, which a practitioner can measure
to predict interpolation's accuracy. A deep translation-equivariant Koopman
autoencoder reproduces the invariant density (\KThreeSMedPct) but not per-mode
resonances (\KThreeOmegaMedRel\ error), so EDMD is our instrument. Every number is
machine-generated and regenerates byte-for-byte.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Extensively chaotic spatiotemporal systems have a thermodynamic limit: as the
domain grows, intensive statistics (Lyapunov-exponent density, correlation
lengths, spectral densities) converge, with finite-size corrections that vanish
as the box grows~\citep{cross1993,egolf1994,manneville1985}. Transfer-operator
(Ruelle--Pollicott, RP) resonances~\citep{ruelle1986,pollicott1985} encode the
statistics that survive to long times in a chaotic system --- correlation-decay
rates, power spectra, mixing --- and are, unlike trajectories, legitimately
predictable past the Lyapunov horizon. This paper asks a scaling question about
them: \emph{does the size-dependence of a learned operator's spectrum converge
smoothly enough that one can fit it on small domains and extrapolate the
statistics of a much larger one, without ever simulating or training at the
large size?}

This connects three threads of our own program: transfer-operator statistics as
the right target for surrogate models of chaos~\citep{howe2026dbar}; long-horizon
statistical (not trajectory) fidelity~\citep{howe2026tpp}; and scaling, rather
than brute-force training, as the route to large systems. Two narrowings keep the
claim honest. \textbf{(N1)} What is predicted at long horizon is
\emph{statistics and slow modes, not trajectories}: $e^{\lambda t}$ spectral
continuation cannot beat a positive Lyapunov exponent, but RP resonances do give
correlation functions, power spectra, and decay rates~\citep{chekroun2020rp1}.
\textbf{(N2)} Koopman/RP spectral \emph{estimation and forecasting} is an
occupied field~\citep{chekroun2020rp1,arbabi2017hankel,das2019delay,williams2015edmd};
we treat it as substrate. The only mechanism we test as novel is
\emph{domain-size extrapolation} of a learned operator's spectrum via a learned
finite-size-scaling flow.

\paragraph{Contributions.}
(i) A characterization (\S\ref{sec:e0}): the finite-size dependence of
EDMD-estimated KS RP/Koopman spectra is smooth and $1/L$-fittable
(\emph{GATE~S passes}); to our knowledge the first such measurement, and the
foundation any FSS flow would stand on. (ii) A working FSS flow (\S\ref{sec:e2})
that does extrapolate the spectrum to $\TargetFactor\times$ the base volume at
single-digit-percent error from zero large-domain data. (iii) \textbf{The honest
verdict}: across two systems chosen to bracket the finite-size convergence rate,
interpolating the largest affordable box beats the flow --- for KS because the
spectrum is converged and the flow is redundant (\S\ref{sec:e3}), and for the
Nikolaevskiy equation, whose marginal mode makes the spectrum converge slowly,
because the flow overshoots the non-monotone drift while the spectral density
stays extensive (\S\ref{sec:e5}). The flow has no advantage window; we say so and
give the mechanism. (iv) \textbf{A diagnostic}: the small-domain spectral drift
(not the correlation length, which is short for both systems) is measurable from
the training data and predicts interpolation's accuracy, so a practitioner can
tell whether extrapolation is trustworthy before paying for a large run.
(v) An honest negative for deep operators (\S\ref{sec:e1}): a translation-
equivariant Koopman autoencoder matches the invariant measure but not per-mode
resonances, so we scale the EDMD operator.

\section{Related work}
\textbf{RP resonances of stochastic/observed systems.}
\citet{chekroun2020rp1,tantet2020rp2,tantet2020rp3} develop reduced-state-space
RP resonances (with an El-Ni\~no application); our substrate is this machinery,
estimated from data. \textbf{Koopman spectral estimation and forecasting.}
EDMD~\citep{williams2015edmd}, delay-coordinate / Hankel
Koopman~\citep{arbabi2017hankel,das2019delay,mezic2005,takens1981}, DMD for
analytic maps~\citep{slipantschuk2020}, and Koopman
autoencoders~\citep{lusch2018,otto2019,azencot2020} are the fixed-domain tools we
build on. \textbf{Domain extension for neural operators} is the named adjacent
problem: \citet{devilleroche2026zeroshot} achieve zero-shot generalization of
transformer neural operators to larger domains via attention locality, and
\citet{huang2025domain} via domain-decomposition operator learning; both are
\emph{locality} routes. Ours is a \emph{spectral / renormalization} route, and
our finding is that a plain wavenumber-interpolation null already matches or beats
the spectral flow in both convergence regimes we tested --- so we cite-compare
rather than reimplement. \textbf{RG $\times$ ML}:
neural-network renormalization group~\citep{li2018nnrg} and analyses of when
learning dynamics renormalize~\citep{zhang2025renormalize} share the RG framing
but target sampling / training dynamics, not operator-spectrum domain extension.
Our inspiration is Wilsonian momentum-shell scaling; empirically the KS data
support only a smooth-in-$1/L$ fit, and we do not oversell the RG language.
Dynamic-RG analyses of the \emph{noisy} KS equation place its long-wavelength
field in the KPZ universality class~\citep{ueno2005noisyks}; that is a different
object (field scaling exponents), not the finite-size dependence of the
transfer-operator spectrum we measure.

\section{Setup}
\label{sec:setup}
We integrate KS, $u_t = -u u_x - u_{xx} - u_{xxxx}$, periodic on $[0,L)$, with a
pseudospectral ETDRK4 scheme~\citep{kassam2005} (2/3 dealiasing), vendored and
regression-pinned against a prior validated implementation~\citep{howe2026dbar}.
The grid resolution $\Delta x = 22/64$ is fixed for all sizes, $dt=0.25$, samples
every $0.5$ time units after a $500$-unit transient; $3$ seeds per size.

\paragraph{Per-sector spectra.}
Translation invariance block-diagonalizes the dynamics over Fourier wavenumbers
$k=2\pi m/L$. For each retained sector ($0<k\le 3$) we form the complex mode
series $\hat u_m(t)/N$ and estimate its leading resonance by Hankel-EDMD: an
$8$-delay dictionary at stride $s$ samples, one-step map advancing by $s$, Grams
accumulated in a single streaming pass over $\{1,8,64,512\}$; the stride whose
window is closest to $3\tau_e(k)$ (with an anti-aliasing guard) is selected. The
leading resonance $\lambda(k)=\log\mu/(s\,\Delta t_s)$ gives decay rate
$\gc(k)=-\mathrm{Re}\,\lambda$ and frequency $\omega(k)=|\mathrm{Im}\,\lambda|$.
We also accumulate a Welch power spectrum; the spectral density is
$\Shat(k)=P_m/(2\pi/L)$, the object with a thermodynamic limit. Over all sizes the
EDMD-implied autocovariance matches the measured one with median $R^2$ between
\MedRSquaredWorst\ and \MedRSquaredBest.

\section{E0: finite-size scaling of KS spectra exists (GATE S)}
\label{sec:e0}
On the common grid $k=2\pi m/22$ (present at every training size) we fit, per
wavenumber, $y(L)=y_\infty + c\,(22/L)^p$ with $p\in\{1,2\}$ chosen by
AICc, seed-averaged and SE-weighted. \textbf{GATE~S} (pre-registered): a smooth
or already-converged size-dependence exists for $\ge 5/\GateSNumK$ wavenumbers
(passed: \GateSSOneGamma/\GateSNumK\ for $\gc$, \GateSSOneS/\GateSNumK\ for
$\Shat$), \emph{and} fitting on $\{22,44,66,88\}$ predicts held-out $L=176$ to
$\le 10\%$ (achieved: \GateSHoldoutGamma\ for $\gc$, \GateSHoldoutS\ for $\Shat$).
GATE~S \textbf{passes}. Finite-size corrections are large at small $L$ --- at
$k\approx0.57$ the decay rate grows from \GammaSmallKSmallL\ ($L=22$) to
\GammaSmallKLargeL\ ($L=88$), a factor \GammaGrowthFactor --- and mostly
saturate by $L=88$; AICc prefers the $1/L^2$ form for
\AiccPowTwoS/\GateSNumK\ of the density points. This smooth, measurable
size-dependence is the paper's foundation (Fig.~\ref{fig:dispersion}).

\section{E1: a deep Koopman operator matches the measure, not the resonances}
\label{sec:e1}
We train a translation-equivariant convolutional Koopman autoencoder (circular
convolutions, a linear circular-convolution propagator, $16$ latent channels) at
$L=22$, $\KThreeTrainMinutes$ min/model. It reproduces the \emph{invariant
spectral density} well --- generated fields match the measured density to
\KThreeSMedPct\ (median) and the two-point correlation to \KThreeCRelLTwo\ --- but
its per-sector \emph{leading resonances} do not match EDMD: even under
generate-and-reestimate (drive the learned stochastic operator, run the identical
estimator used on data), the decay rates are off by \KThreeGammaMedRel\ and the
oscillation frequencies by \KThreeOmegaMedRel. The variance-dominated
reconstruction loss pins the measure without pinning each mode's resonant phase.
Per our pre-registered kill condition K3, we therefore build the FSS flow on the
data-driven Koopman (EDMD) spectrum --- itself a learned transfer operator --- and
report the deep operator as this measure-fidelity/resonance-infidelity result.

\section{E2: the finite-size-scaling flow works}
\label{sec:e2}
The FSS flow generalizes the GATE-S recipe to arbitrary target wavenumbers:
for each target $k$, gather the value at the nearest mode of each training size
and fit the seed-averaged $1/L$ (or $1/L^2$, AICc) model, extrapolating to the
target $L$. We report the headline over the \emph{full-support band}
$k\in[2\pi/22,2.2]$ (wavenumbers present at all four training sizes); the
partial-support band $[2\pi/88,2\pi/22)$ and the genuinely new long-wave modes
($k<2\pi/88$) are reported separately.

Fitting on $\{22,44,66,88\}$ and extrapolating with \emph{zero} large-domain
data, the flow predicts the $L=1408$ spectrum ($\TargetSectors$ sectors,
$\TargetFactor\times$ the base volume): decay rates to \FlowTargetGamma, spectral
density to \FlowTargetSPct, band-limited two-point correlation to \FlowTargetC,
correlation times to \FlowTargetTau, and a slow-mode-subspace Jaccard overlap of
\FlowTargetSlow\ (Fig.~\ref{fig:headline}). The new long-wave band is revealing:
its \emph{spectral density} extrapolates almost perfectly (\FlowTargetNewSPct\
over \FlowTargetNewModes\ modes, since $\Shat$ is flat at low $k$) but its
\emph{decay rates} do not (\FlowTargetNewGamma\ error) --- these hydrodynamic
modes are far slower than anything in the training set, so the dynamics, unlike
the statics, cannot be extrapolated in wavenumber. Taken alone this is a positive
domain-extension result; \S\ref{sec:e3} shows why it is nonetheless unnecessary.

\section{E3: KS --- when interpolation suffices (K2 fires)}
\label{sec:e3}
The flow works, but does it \emph{beat} the obvious no-flow null? For KS, no ---
and understanding why gives half the criterion. We compare the flow against the
strongest no-flow null: \textbf{interp-88},
which interpolates the measured $L=88$ spectrum in wavenumber to the target grid
with \emph{no size flow at all} (the spectral analog of evaluating a small-domain
operator on a larger domain, the locality/zero-shot
route~\citep{devilleroche2026zeroshot}). Table~\ref{tab:baselines} is decisive:
interp-88 predicts the $L=1408$ decay rates to \InterpEightEightGamma, density to
\InterpEightEightSPct, and correlations to \InterpEightEightC\ --- \emph{better}
than the flow on all \KTwoNumMetrics\ headline metrics. Our pre-registered
kill condition \textbf{K2 fires} (the flow wins \KTwoFlowWins/\KTwoNumMetrics):
the FSS mechanism adds nothing over translation-invariant interpolation for KS.

The mechanism is fast convergence (\S\ref{sec:e4}, Table~\ref{tab:convergence}):
the KS bulk spectrum is within \ConvEightEightGamma\ (decay rates) and
\ConvEightEightS\ ($\Shat$) of the $L=1408$ limit \emph{already at $L=88$}, so
using the largest affordable box directly beats extrapolating a $1/L$ flow, which
over-corrects past the already-converged largest size. The weaker strict-tiling
null (periodically tile the $L=22$ field) captures band-integrated power to
\TilingBandPower\ but fails on the two-point correlation (\TilingC) through the
spurious $L=22$ periodicity it imposes --- a reminder that tiling, unlike
smooth $k$-interpolation, injects artificial long-range order. A flow fit on an
aggressive base $\{22,33,44\}$ is worse still (\SmallBaseGamma\ on decay rates;
its density extrapolation is numerically unstable), and even there interp-44
wins. With \emph{some} large-domain data, direct EDMD at the target reaches
\EdmdLimLongGamma\ (decay rates) from only $10{,}000$ time units --- and the full
oracle costs \OracleTargetMultiple$\times$ our small-domain compute, while
interp-88 costs the same as the flow.

\section{E4: boundaries --- where each route breaks}
\label{sec:e4}
\textbf{Convergence: the diagnostic that matters.}
Table~\ref{tab:convergence} reports the median distance of each size's common-grid
spectrum from the $L=1408$ limit --- the \emph{spectral finite-size drift}. The
KS decay rates are within \ConvFourFourGamma\ by $L=44$ and \ConvEightEightGamma\
by $L=88$; this rapid convergence is exactly why interp-88 suffices and the flow
is redundant. The drift is measurable from the training sizes alone, so it, rather
than any post-hoc knowledge, tells the practitioner interpolation is safe here.
One might expect the real-space correlation length to be the diagnostic instead;
it is not. The KS integral correlation length is $\xi\approx\KSXiEightEight$ (and
$C(r)$ is negligible beyond $r\approx40$), but so is Nikolaevskiy's
($\NikXiEightEight$, \S\ref{sec:e5}) --- yet the two converge at completely
different rates. What separates them is a marginal mode, not a long correlation
length; the drift captures its effect directly.

\textbf{Boundary conditions.} Under odd-parity (Dirichlet-type $u=u_{xx}=0$)
boundaries at $L=176$, the bulk two-point correlation heals to the periodic
prediction within a wall layer of \OddHealingLength\ units; the periodic FSS-flow
prediction reproduces the odd-parity \emph{bulk} correlation to \OddCVsFlow\ and
the bulk-point temporal autocorrelation to \OddAcfVsFlow, i.e.\ the size-scaled
periodic spectrum transfers to the bulk of a non-periodic domain.

\textbf{Scale content.} Splitting $L=1408$ into an energy-containing band
($k\le1.5$) and a microscale band ($k>1.5$), the flow's decay-rate error is
\BandEnergyGamma\ in the energy band and \BandMicroGamma\ in the microscale band.

\textbf{New long-wave modes.} The genuinely new modes ($k<2\pi/88$, absent at
every training size) separate statics from dynamics cleanly
(Fig.~\ref{fig:lowk}): their spectral density extrapolates in wavenumber to
\FlowTargetNewSPct\ (the density is flat at low $k$), but their decay rates,
being far slower than anything trained, are not recovered (\FlowTargetNewGamma).
No method --- flow or null --- predicts the new modes' \emph{dynamics}; this is the
honest ceiling of size-and-wavenumber extrapolation.

\section{E5: Nikolaevskiy --- a slow-converging spectrum, and the flow still loses}
\label{sec:e5}
KS converges so fast that interpolation is near-exact; does the flow earn its keep
when the spectrum converges \emph{slowly}? We test the hardest honest case:
the Nikolaevskiy equation,
$u_t = -u\,u_x + \mathcal{F}^{-1}\!\big[\sigma(k)\,\hat u\big]$ with linear growth
rate $\sigma(k)=k^2[\,r-(1-k^2)^2\,]$. The prefactor $k^2$ makes $k{=}0$ \emph{exactly
marginal} --- a conservation-law/Goldstone mode --- giving soft-mode
turbulence~\citep{nikolaevskiy1989,tribelsky1996,matthews2000} in which the
marginal mode renormalizes the low-$k$ decay rates with large, slow finite-size
corrections. We run it at $r=\NikR$ with the identical integrator family,
estimators, and flow (ladder $\{44,66,88\}$; $L=22$ is a turbulence-onset outlier
at this $r$ and is excluded). The effect is real and large: the decay-rate drift is
still \NikConvEightEight\ at $L=88$ (Fig.~\ref{fig:criterion}, left), roughly
$8\times$ KS's \ConvEightEightGamma, so \textbf{interpolation is genuinely
degraded} --- interp-88 misses the $L=\NikTargetL$ decay rates by \NikInterpGamma\
(versus \InterpEightEightGamma\ for KS). Notably the real-space correlation length
is \emph{not} what changed: it is $\xi\approx\NikXiEightEight$, essentially the
same short value as KS. The slow convergence is a spectral effect of the marginal
mode, not a long correlation length.

This is the regime the flow was built for --- and it still loses. Extrapolating the
flow from $\{44,66,88\}$ to $L=\NikTargetL$ ($\NikSectors$ sectors, zero
large-domain data) gives decay rates to \NikFlowGamma\ --- \emph{worse} than the
degraded interp-88 (\NikInterpGamma) --- and spectral density to \NikFlowSPct\
versus interp-88's \NikInterpSPct. The flow beats the best no-flow null on
\NikFlowWins/5 metrics. Two mechanisms defeat it, both visible in the data. First,
the spectral density is \emph{extensive} even here: $\Shat(k)$ is converged by
$L=44$, so interpolation is near-exact for it (\NikInterpSPct) while the flow only
adds extrapolation noise (\NikFlowSPct). Second, the finite-size drift that does
exist --- in the low-$k$ decay rates --- is \emph{non-monotone} across the training
sizes (soft-mode-contaminated, and estimated from finite trajectories), so the
$1/L$ fit, extrapolated over an $8\times$ span in system size, overshoots; interp-88,
which does no extrapolation, is closer despite being biased. The flow needs a clean
monotone approach to a limit, and the marginal-mode regime does not provide one.
So across both systems --- fast-converging (KS) and slow-converging (Nikolaevskiy)
--- interpolating the largest affordable box beats the $1/L$ flow.

\section{What we do not claim, and where the flow might yet help}
We do not claim trajectory forecasting (N1): only statistics and slow modes. We do
\emph{not} claim the FSS flow beats interpolation --- for neither KS nor
Nikolaevskiy does it, and the honest reading is that a $1/L$ finite-size-scaling
flow has no advantage window over interpolating the largest affordable box for
these 1-D operator spectra. We make no 2-D/3-D or turbulence claims (none were run)
and no claim about universality classes. We do not claim the deep operator captures
resonances (it does not). We do not claim the new long-wave modes' \emph{dynamics}
are predicted --- only their spectral density.

\paragraph{Why the flow lost, and the one regime that could reverse it.} The flow
is redundant when the spectrum converges fast (interpolation is then near-exact)
and it overshoots when convergence is slow but \emph{non-monotone} (as the
marginal-mode regime makes it). The gap it would need is a system whose spectrum
converges \emph{slowly but cleanly and monotonically} in $1/L$ --- extensive, so a
thermodynamic limit exists to extrapolate to, yet far from it at the largest
affordable box. Neither of our two systems is that: KS converges too fast, and
Nikolaevskiy's slow convergence is non-monotone and confined to a few soft modes
while its density stays extensive. Whether such a clean-slow-convergence system
exists (long-range coupling and size-dependent instabilities are the candidates we
could not rule out) is the open question our negative result sharpens. Until one is
exhibited, the practical guidance is concrete: measure the small-domain spectral
drift; if it has saturated, interpolate the largest box; if it has not, no
fixed-form extrapolation we tested is trustworthy --- acquire large-domain data.

\section{Reproducibility}
Every number above is emitted by \texttt{gen\_paper\_numbers.py} from committed
JSON and regenerates byte-for-byte (\texttt{verify\_regen.py}); the KS and
Nikolaevskiy integrators, the streaming estimators, and the scaling fits are
unit-tested (spectra vs.\ analytic linear sectors; KS integrator regression
against a prior validated code; Nikolaevskiy marginal-mode, mean-conservation and
chaos checks; scaling round-trip). Both systems share one ETDRK4 core and one
system-agnostic estimator/flow pipeline --- only the linear symbol changes ---
which is what makes the KS-vs-Nikolaevskiy comparison a controlled one. All runs
use a fixed grid, $3$ seeds, and pre-registered gates (PLAN.md). Code and data:
\texttt{DOI to be assigned at submission}. Recency re-sweep logged in PLAN.md.
