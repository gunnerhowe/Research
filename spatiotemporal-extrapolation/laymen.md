# In plain terms

## The question

Some physical systems are chaotic *and* spread out in space — a turbulent fluid, a
flame front, ripples on a long channel. You cannot predict their exact future
(chaos erases that), but you *can* predict their **statistics**: how fast patterns
lose memory of themselves, what the typical wavelengths are, how long correlations
last. Those statistics are encoded in the "spectrum" of a mathematical object
called the transfer operator.

Simulating such a system on a **big** domain is expensive. So we asked: can we
learn the operator's spectrum on a few **small** domains, watch how it changes as
the domain grows, and **extrapolate** to a much bigger domain — without ever
simulating the big one? This "finite-size scaling" trick is standard in physics;
the question is whether it beats the obvious shortcut.

## What we found

**The finite-size-scaling flow works — but it never beats the obvious shortcut.**

The obvious shortcut is: just take the spectrum of the **biggest small box you can
afford** and stretch it onto the big domain, with no clever scaling at all. We
compared our flow against that shortcut on two very different systems, and the
shortcut won both times:

- **Kuramoto–Sivashinsky** settles into its large-size behavior *fast* — by a box
  of size 88 it's already within a few percent of the size-1408 answer. So the
  shortcut is nearly exact (3%), and our flow (10%), while it genuinely works, is
  just **unnecessary** — there's nothing left for a scaling law to correct.

- **Nikolaevskiy** is the hard case we built specifically to break the shortcut: it
  has a special "marginal mode" that makes its spectrum converge *slowly*, so the
  biggest affordable box really is *not* converged and the shortcut is genuinely
  worse (16%). This is exactly where a scaling law should shine. But our flow did
  **worse still** (21%): part of the spectrum (the power at each wavelength)
  converges quickly anyway, and the part that doesn't converge drifts
  *unevenly* from size to size, so extrapolating a smooth "1/size" trend across a
  big gap **overshoots**. Not extrapolating at all turned out to be safer.

So the honest headline is a **negative result**: for these systems, a simple 1/size
scaling flow has no regime where it beats just using your biggest box. It's
redundant when the spectrum converges fast, and it overshoots when it converges
slowly-but-unevenly.

## Why that's still worth publishing

Negative results that are *clean and explained* save the field real effort. We
deliver three things: (1) the first measurement that these learned operator spectra
scale smoothly with domain size (the thing a scaling law would need); (2) a working
— if redundant — recipe to extrapolate them, tested to 64× with no big-domain data;
and (3) a **practical diagnostic**: before trusting any extrapolation, measure how
much the spectrum still *drifts* between your small boxes. If it's stopped drifting,
just use your biggest box. If it hasn't, no fixed scaling rule we tried is
trustworthy — go get big-domain data.

We also point to the one situation that *could* rescue the flow — a system whose
spectrum converges slowly but *smoothly and evenly* — which neither of our two
systems does, and which we flag as the open question.
